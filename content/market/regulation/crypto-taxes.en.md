## Crypto Taxes: General Principles

---

Crypto gives you financial freedom. But it doesn't cancel taxes.

A lot of people think: "crypto is anonymous, the taxman won't know." That's a dangerous myth. Exchanges hand over data to regulators, the blockchain is public, and tax authorities are tracking crypto assets more aggressively than ever.

> **Reality:** crypto taxes aren't a matter of "if" — they're a matter of "when."

In this article we'll cover:

>- what transactions are taxable;
>- what a taxable event is;
>- how and where to pay taxes;
>- and which countries are the most crypto-friendly.

---

## Crypto Is an Asset. Taxes Are a Fact of Life

Legally speaking, most countries treat cryptocurrency as **property** or **an asset** — not as money.

That means:

>- if you just bought crypto and hold it — no tax;
>- if you sell it, trade it, or earn it as income — there's almost always a tax.

> **The golden rule:** tax doesn't kick in when you buy — it kicks in when you **exit** crypto into fiat or receive crypto as income.

---

## When Tax Applies: Taxable Events

### Selling Crypto for Fiat

The most obvious case. You sold Bitcoin for dollars, euros, or rubles — you made a profit (or a loss). You pay tax on the profit.

### Swapping One Crypto for Another

In some countries (like the US) **trading BTC for ETH** is also a taxable event. Even if you never cashed out to fiat.

The calculation works like this:

> **tax = (value of the coin received − cost of the coin spent) × tax rate**

### Receiving Crypto as Income

This includes:

>- salary paid in crypto;
>- staking and mining rewards;
>- airdrops;
>- DeFi income (lending, farming).

The rate depends on the country, but almost everywhere this is taxed as **ordinary income** (income tax), not as capital gains.

---

## When No Tax Applies: Non-Taxable Events

Some operations **don't trigger** a tax:

>- buying crypto with fiat (you're just entering the asset);
>- transferring crypto between your own wallets;
>- holding (HODL);
>- gifts (within limits — depends on the country).

---

## How Tax Is Calculated: Realized Gains

You don't pay tax on "paper gains" — only on **realized gains**.

Example:

> 1. You bought 1 BTC for $20,000.
> 2. A year later BTC is at $60,000.
> 3. You sold — tax is due on the $40,000 difference.
>
> If you hadn't sold — no tax.

This is called **capital gains tax**. Rates depend on how long you held:

>- **short-term** (under 1 year) — higher (like ordinary income);
>- **long-term** (over 1 year) — lower (preferential rate).

---

## Where to Pay Taxes: Residency Principle

You pay taxes where you're a **tax resident** — not where the exchange or wallet is located.

> **If you're a Russian tax resident** — you report to the Russian tax service even if you trade on Binance and live in Thailand on a tourist visa.

Tax residency is determined by:

>- days spent in the country (usually 183 days a year);
>- where your center of vital interests is;
>- citizenship (only in the US and Eritrea).

---

## Country-by-Country Breakdown

### United States

Some of the strictest rules in the world:

>- every sale or swap is a taxable event;
>- staking and mining income — ordinary income (up to 37%);
>- long-term capital gains — up to 20%;
>- all transactions must be reported;
>- even buying coffee with crypto counts as a sale.

> **Tip:** in the US, track every single transaction from day one. Penalties for non-reporting are massive.

### Germany

One of the most friendly countries for long-term holders:

>- **0% tax** on capital gains if you hold crypto **over 1 year**;
>- selling within a year — taxed at income tax rates (up to 45%);
>- staking income is taxable, but if held over a year — also tax-free.

> **Important:** this rule only applies to crypto you bought with your own money. Mining and staking are treated separately.

### Portugal

For a long time Portugal was a "tax haven" for crypto — zero tax on crypto gains.

Things changed in 2023:

>- capital gains on crypto held **under 1 year** — 28%;
>- held **over 1 year** — 0% (the exemption stays);
>- mining and staking income — 25% to 50%.

> **Bottom line:** Portugal is still great for HODLers, but not for traders.

### Russia

Crypto is legal in Russia, but regulated:

>- since 2024 — crypto can be legally sold through an **Experimental Legal Regime (ELR)**;
>- **personal income tax (PIT) of 13–15%** on profits from sales;
>- mining is legalized — taxed as entrepreneurial activity;
>- crypto assets above a certain threshold must be declared (final law still pending).

> **Catch:** before 2024, Russia had no clear framework for paying crypto taxes. The rules are getting clearer now.

## How to Pay Taxes: Step-by-Step

The process is similar in most countries:

1. **Track every transaction** — buys, sells, swaps. Use tax calculators like CoinTracker, Koinly, or CoinTracking.

2. **Calculate your profit** — sales minus purchases. Many countries let you deduct losses (tax loss harvesting).

3. **File your return** — by the deadline set by local law.

4. **Pay the tax** — through your bank or the tax authority.

> **Important:** if you don't file, the risks vary by country. In the US it's a criminal offense. In some countries it's just a fine.

---

## Conclusion

Crypto taxes aren't scary. They're just part of the game.

The key is understanding the basics:

>- tax applies when you **sell, swap, or earn income**;
>- the rate depends on **how long you held** and **where you live**;
>- the easiest places are those with **long-term exemptions** (0% after a year);
>- keep records — it'll save you money and headaches.

> **Crypto gives you freedom. But taxes are responsibility. One doesn't work without the other.**

## FAQ

### Do I need to pay tax if I just bought crypto and haven't sold?

No. Tax only kicks in on realization — selling, swapping, or earning income.

### Is swapping one crypto for another taxable?

In most countries — yes. In the US and Germany — definitely yes. In Russia — the rules aren't fully settled yet.

### How do I figure out how much tax I owe?

Use services like Koinly, CoinTracker, or CoinTracking. They connect to your wallets and exchanges and calculate your profits automatically.

### What happens if I don't pay crypto taxes?

Depends on the country. From fines to criminal liability. In the US, non-reporting can mean up to 5 years in prison. In some countries — account freezes.

### Can I reduce my crypto tax through losses?

Yes. Many countries let you offset crypto sale losses against your gains. This is called tax loss harvesting.
