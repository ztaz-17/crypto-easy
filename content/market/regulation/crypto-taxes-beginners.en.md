## Crypto Taxes for Beginners: Where to Start

---

Crypto taxes — a topic that makes many beginners' heads spin. "Do I even have to pay?", "What if I just bought and held?", "What happens if I don't pay?" — sound familiar?

In this article, I'll break it all down. No complex legal jargon, just simple examples. You'll learn where to start, how to keep records, and how to avoid fines.

> **Key takeaway:** crypto taxes aren't scary when you understand the rules. Ignoring them — that's what's truly scary.

---

## Where Do You Even Start?

The most common question: "I just bought a little Bitcoin — do I need to do something right now?"

Short answer: If you **bought and held** — no. Tax kicks in when you **realize a profit**, not when you buy. But you should start tracking from day one.

Here's what to do right now if you're already trading:

1. **Set up a tracking spreadsheet** — Google Sheets or Excel. Log every trade: date, purchase amount, sale amount, exchange rate.
2. **Save screenshots** — of orders, transfers between wallets, transaction confirmations.
3. **Connect a crypto tax calculator** — services like Koinly, CoinTracking, or CoinLedger pull data directly from exchanges.
4. **Find out your country's filing deadline** — they're different everywhere.

Sounds like extra work? Trust me, reconstructing two years of trades from memory is a hundred times harder.

## What Transactions Are Taxable: Plain and Simple

Let's go through the most common situations beginners run into.

### You Sold Crypto for Fiat

This is the clearest case. You bought 1 BTC for $30,000, sold for $45,000 — the $15,000 difference is your **profit**. You pay tax on it.

Simple formula:

> **Tax = (Sale Price − Purchase Price) × Tax Rate**

The rate depends on your country. In the US it's up to 37% for short-term, 20% for long-term. In Germany it's 0% if held over a year. In Russia it's 13–15%.

### You Swapped One Crypto for Another

Traded BTC for ETH? Or USDT for SOL? In most countries (US, Germany, most EU nations) this is also a **taxable event**.

The tax is calculated as if you first sold BTC for dollars, then bought ETH. You need to check: did BTC go up from the time you bought it to the time you swapped it?

Example:
- You bought 1 BTC for $30,000
- A month later you swapped it for ETH when BTC was $38,000
- Profit: $8,000 — you owe tax on that
- Your cost basis for ETH is $38,000 — that's your starting point for future taxes

### You Received Crypto as Income

This includes:
- Salary paid in crypto
- Staking rewards (Ethereum 2.0, Solana, etc.)
- Mining
- [Airdrops](/en/glossary/#airdrop)
- [DeFi](/en/glossary/#defi) yields (lending, farming)

All of this counts as **income** and is taxed just like your regular salary. The rate is your ordinary income tax rate.

### You Bought an NFT or Played GameFi

If you bought an [NFT](/en/glossary/#nft) with crypto — that may be a taxable event (depends on the country). If you sold the [NFT](/en/glossary/#nft) for more than you paid — the profit is taxable.

In GameFi (games like Axie Infinity), token rewards are considered income when you receive them.

## What Transactions Are NOT Taxable

Not everything triggers a tax bill:

| Transaction | Tax? | Explanation |
|------------|------|-------------|
| Just bought and holding ([HODL](/en/glossary/#hodl)) | ❌ No | Until you sell — no tax |
| Transfer between your own wallets | ❌ No | Moving from exchange to [cold wallet](/en/glossary/#cold-wallet) isn't a sale |
| Gifted crypto (within limits) | ❌ No in some countries | Varies by country |
| Bought crypto and lost access | ❌ No | It's a loss, not profit |

## How to Calculate Profit: Two Main Methods

This is where beginners get confused most often. You bought BTC several times at different prices — $30,000, then $45,000. How do you calculate tax when you sell?

### FIFO (First In, First Out)

You're assumed to sell your oldest coins first. This is the default method in the US and many other countries.

Example:
1. Bought 0.5 BTC at $30,000
2. Bought 0.5 BTC at $50,000
3. Sold 0.5 BTC at $60,000
4. FIFO: you sold the ones bought at $30,000 → profit of $30,000

### LIFO (Last In, First Out)

You're assumed to sell your most recent coins first. Some countries let you choose.

Same numbers:
- LIFO: you sold the ones bought at $50,000 → profit of only $10,000 → lower tax!

> **Important:** each country has its own rules. In the US you can choose (specific identification), in Russia it's not clearly regulated. Check with a local tax advisor.

## How to Use Losses: Tax Loss Harvesting

This is probably the most useful tool for minimizing taxes. If you sold one crypto at a loss and another at a gain, you can **deduct the losses from the gains**.

Example:
- Bought SOL for $100, sold for $30 → $70 loss
- Bought ETH for $2,000, sold for $3,000 → $1,000 gain
- Tax is paid on $1,000 − $70 = $930

Many countries let you carry losses forward to future years if you had no gains this year. This is called tax loss harvesting, and even professional traders use it actively.

### When's the Best Time to Sell at a Loss?

Usually near the end of the year — November through December. Sell your "bad" coins at a loss, lock in the loss, reduce your tax on "good" coins. If you want to stay in the position — buy them back a couple days later (careful: the US has a wash sale rule, most countries don't).

## What Documents to Gather for Tax Filing

Prepare in advance, and tax filing won't be scary.

The minimum set of documents you should have ready:

1. **Exchange trade history** — a CSV file with dates, trading pairs, volumes, and prices. Every major exchange (Binance, Bybit, OKX, Kraken) can export this.
2. **Fiat deposit/withdrawal confirmations** — bank statements, screenshots of card-to-exchange transfers.
3. **Staking and mining records** — how much and when you received rewards, the exchange rate at that time.
4. **P2P transaction records** — if you bought crypto directly from people (Binance P2P, LocalBitcoins), save screenshots of the listings.
5. **Tax calculator report** — if you use Koinly, CoinTracking, etc., they generate ready-to-file reports for the IRS, HMRC, or your local tax authority.

### How Long to Keep Documents?

At least 3 years after filing. In some countries, the statute of limitations for tax audits is up to 6 years. Better safe than sorry.

## Common Beginner Mistakes in Crypto Taxes

Here's what usually goes wrong:

### Mistake 1: "Crypto is anonymous, the taxman won't know"

This is the most dangerous myth. All major exchanges share data with regulators. Binance, Coinbase, Kraken — all comply with [KYC](/en/glossary/#kyc). If you withdrew money to your bank — the bank already knows. The [blockchain](/en/glossary/#blockchain) is a public ledger, and specialized software tracks suspicious transactions.

### Mistake 2: "I only swapped BTC for USDT — that's not a sale"

Technically, crypto-to-crypto swaps count as a sale in most countries. You sold BTC and bought USDT. If BTC went up since you bought it — you owe tax.

### Mistake 3: "I took a loss, so I don't need to file"

Even if you made no profit, many countries still require you to file a return (or file showing the loss) so you can carry it forward. Miss the deadline and you lose the ability to offset future gains.

### Mistake 4: "Moving crypto to my cold wallet is a sale"

No. Transfers between your own wallets are not taxable events. But keep proof that both wallets are yours — the tax authority may ask.

### Mistake 5: "I'll file when the price goes up"

You pay tax based on when you made the trade, not on future expectations. If you sold this year, file by the deadline (e.g., April 15 in the US, April 30 in Russia, October 31 in the UK).

## When to Hire a Professional

You don't always have to do it all yourself. Here are signs it's time to see a tax advisor:

- You have **more than 100 trades** in a year
- You trade on **multiple exchanges** and use **DeFi protocols**
- You received **crypto income** (salary, staking, mining)
- You **live in one country but trade from another**
- You have a complex portfolio with **NFTs, DeFi, and yield farming**
- The tax authority already sent you **a letter with questions**

A good crypto-savvy tax advisor will save you more money than their fee. Especially if you're in the US, where mistakes can cost thousands in penalties.

### Where to Find One?

- Specialized crypto accounting firms (CryptoTax, CoinLedger partners)
- Tax consultants listed on crypto exchange websites
- Recommendations from crypto communities (but verify reviews)

## Conclusion

Crypto taxes are nothing to panic about. They're just part of financial literacy — like knowing how to budget your income.

Key takeaways for beginners:

1. **Tax kicks in when you sell, swap, or earn income** — not when you buy.
2. **Losses can be deducted from gains** — track losing trades too.
3. **Automate your tracking** — tax calculators save hours of work.
4. **Don't ignore the tax authority** — sooner or later, the data reaches them.
5. **When in doubt, ask a professional.** A consultation costs less than a fine.

> **Remember:** taxes are the price of civilization. Crypto doesn't cancel your obligations — it just gives you more control over your finances. Use that control wisely.
