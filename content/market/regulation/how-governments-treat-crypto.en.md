## How Governments Treat Crypto Around the World

---

Crypto doesn't give a damn about borders. Bitcoin doesn't care what country you're in — a transaction moves just as fast between New York and Tokyo.

But governments? They care a lot.

Every country decides for itself: legalize crypto, ban it, or try to squeeze it into the existing financial system.

Here are five completely different approaches:

>- **USA** — regulation through courts and the SEC;
>- **EU** — one law for the entire union;
>- **UAE / Dubai** — a crypto-friendly jurisdiction;
>- **Singapore** — clear rules and licenses;
>- **China** — a total ban (but it's not that simple).

> **Key takeaway:** how a government treats crypto directly affects how safe and profitable it is to use in that country.

---

## USA: Regulation Through the SEC

America is the biggest crypto market in the world. And yet there's no single federal crypto law.

Instead, regulation is handled by different agencies:

- **SEC** (Securities and Exchange Commission) — considers most cryptocurrencies to be securities;
- **CFTC** (Commodity Futures Trading Commission) — treats Bitcoin and Ether as commodities;
- **FinCEN** (Financial Crimes Enforcement Network) — regulates exchanges.

In 2024, the SEC approved **spot Bitcoin and Ether ETFs** — a historic move that gave people access to crypto through traditional exchanges.

But at the same time:

>- the SEC is actively suing crypto companies (Binance, Coinbase, Ripple);
>- banks are not allowed to hold crypto directly on their balance sheets;
>- mining is regulated at the state level (Texas — 歡迎, New York — not so much).

**Bottom line:** crypto is legal in the US, but the regulatory environment stays uncertain. Companies spend millions on lawyers.

## European Union: MiCA — One Law to Rule Them All

Europe took a different path.

Instead of dealing with each crypto company through the courts, the EU passed a single regulation — **MiCA (Markets in Crypto-Assets)** — which came into force in 2024–2025.

What MiCA brings:

- one set of rules for all 27 EU countries;
- licensing for crypto exchanges and wallets;
- consumer protection;
- mandatory reserves for stablecoins.

> **The upside:** if a company gets a license in one EU country, it can operate across the entire union.

**MiCA's downsides:**

- heavy bureaucracy for small projects;
- strict reporting requirements;
- stablecoins are essentially treated like bank deposits.

**Bottom line:** the EU created a predictable environment. Companies know the rules, but the entry bar is high.

---

## UAE and Dubai: A Crypto-Friendly Jurisdiction

The United Arab Emirates went with an attraction strategy.

Dubai set up **VARA** (Virtual Assets Regulatory Authority) — the world's first dedicated virtual asset regulator. Abu Dhabi has **FSRA** (Financial Services Regulatory Authority).

What the UAE did:

- created **crypto-free zones** (like the Dubai Multi Commodities Centre);
- allowed crypto payments for real estate and services;
- launched its own crypto exchange (tied to local authorities);
- attracted major crypto companies (Binance, Crypto.com, OKX).

> **Key point:** the UAE has low taxes on crypto profits, and free zones offer zero taxes.

But there are catches:

- businesses need a license (not cheap to get);
- strict KYC and AML rules (no anonymity);
- privacy coins are banned (Monero, Zcash).

**Bottom line:** the UAE is one of the most convenient countries for crypto business — but only if you're willing to operate above board.

---

## Singapore: Clear Rules

Singapore is another example of a pro-crypto jurisdiction, but with a different approach. No "free zones" here — just **one unified law** and a strict regulator, MAS (Monetary Authority of Singapore).

Key things to know:

- all crypto services need a license under the Payment Services Act;
- getting licensed is hard and expensive (hundreds of thousands of dollars);
- crypto advertising to retail investors is restricted;
- stablecoins have their own separate rules.

> **The upside:** a license in Singapore is a badge of quality.

**Downsides:**

- high entry barrier;
- tough capital and governance requirements;
- local banks are reluctant to work with crypto companies.

**Bottom line:** Singapore is for big players who are ready to pay for reputation.

---

## China: A Total Ban, But...

China is the toughest example of regulation.

In 2021, China declared:
- all crypto transactions illegal;
- mining — outlawed;
- exchanges — blocked.

But there are important nuances:

- China has **its own digital currency** — e-CNY (digital yuan), fully controlled by the central bank;
- many Chinese citizens still trade crypto through P2P platforms and VPNs;
- Chinese companies hold significant amounts of Bitcoin (through offshore entities);
- the ban didn't stop China from being a major player in blockchain technology (patents, research).

> **The paradox:** China banned crypto for its citizens but keeps developing the technology at the state level.

**Bottom line:** de jure — a total ban; de facto — a gray zone for those willing to take risks.

## Conclusion

There's no single global approach to crypto. Every country picks its own strategy:

- **USA** — big market, regulatory chaos;
- **EU** — predictability and bureaucracy;
- **UAE** — friendliness and low taxes;
- **Singapore** — strictness and reputation;
- **China** — a ban and its own digital yuan.

What does this mean for the average user?

> **If you just hold crypto in a [non-custodial wallet](/en/glossary/#non-custodial-wallet)** — the government barely touches you. Nobody can freeze your funds.

But if you:

- buy crypto on an exchange;
- sell crypto for fiat;
- run a crypto business;

> **then your country's laws are the rules of the game.**

Golden rule: check the regulation in your country before making any moves. Ignorance of the law doesn't get you off the hook — especially in the crypto world.

## FAQ

### Which country has the lowest crypto taxes?
UAE (Dubai), Singapore (0% capital gains tax), Portugal (no tax until 2023, now 28% if held under a year).

### Is it legal to buy crypto in China?
Officially — no. Crypto transactions have been banned there since 2021.

### What is MiCA in simple terms?
The EU's single crypto law that sets rules for exchanges, wallets, and stablecoins across the entire European Union.

### Which country was the first to legalize Bitcoin?
Japan — in 2017, it recognized Bitcoin as a legal payment method.

### Does regulation affect Bitcoin's price?
Yes, directly. News about bans usually pushes the price down, while ETF approvals or legalization announcements tend to push it up.
