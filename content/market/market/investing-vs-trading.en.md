## Investing vs Trading: What Should a Beginner Choose

---

You got into crypto. Bought some bitcoin. Now you sit there checking the chart every 5 minutes.

Price goes up — euphoria.
Price goes down — panic.

Sound familiar?

You've got a choice: **invest in crypto** for the long haul, or **trade** — make money on short-term price movements.

Trading feels fast and profitable. Investing feels boring and slow.

But reality is more complicated.

In this article we'll cover:

>- how investing and trading differ in crypto;
>- the risks of each approach;
>- why the **[DCA](/en/glossary/#dca) (dollar-cost averaging) strategy** is the best starting point for beginners;
>- and how to read Japanese candlesticks to understand the market.

---

## Investing: Buy and Hold (HODL)

Investing is an approach where you:

- buy an asset (bitcoin, ether);
- hold it for **months or years**;
- don't react to short-term fluctuations.

### The HODL Philosophy

The term **[HODL](/en/glossary/#hodl)** was born in 2013 when a user on the Bitcointalk forum drunkenly typed "I AM HODLING" instead of "holding." The word stuck and became a symbol of the long-term holder movement.

HODL isn't about laziness. It's about **confidence** that the asset will grow over several years, despite temporary dips.

### Pros of Investing

>- no need to watch charts 24/7;
>- less stress and fewer emotional decisions;
>- historically, bitcoin rises on **4-year cycles** (halving → growth);
>- taxes are simpler (fewer transactions).

### Cons of Investing

>- your money is locked up for years;
>- when the market drops 70–80%, you need strong nerves;
>- returns are lower than successful trading (but so are the risks).

## Trading: Buying and Selling on Short Timeframes

Trading is active buying and selling. You buy and sell an asset within:

- minutes (**scalping**);
- hours (**day trading**);
- days or weeks (**swing trading**).

The goal is to profit from **price movement**, whether the market is going up or down.

### Pros of Trading

>- you can make money on the way down (shorting);
>- fast results — profit is visible immediately;
>- your money isn't locked up for years.

### Cons of Trading

>- **high stress** — every price move hits your nerves;
>- requires deep knowledge (chart analysis, indicators, risk management);
>- most beginners **lose money** in their first months;
>- needs a lot of time — it's a full-time job;
>- psychological pressure (FOMO, panic, greed).

> **Stat check:** 80–90% of retail traders lose money. That's not a myth — it's exchange data.

---

## Risks: Why Trading Is Harder

### The Emotional Trap

When you trade, your decisions are constantly driven by emotions:

- price shoots up — **FOMO** ([Fear Of Missing Out](/en/glossary/#fomo)), fear of missing profit — you buy at the top;
- price drops — **panic** — you sell at a loss;
- after a loss — **the urge to get even** — even bigger losses.

With investing, there are fewer of these traps because you just hold the asset.

### Leverage and Margin Trading

Many exchanges offer **leverage** (x5, x10, x50 and higher). It multiplies both profit and loss.

Leverage is strongly not recommended for beginners:

- one 2% move and your position is liquidated (at x50 leverage);
- your money gets wiped out instantly;
- after losing 50%, you need to earn **+100%** just to break even.

> **Golden rule:** never use leverage until you understand the market. Better yet — don't use it at all.

## Approaches: DCA for Beginners

### What Is DCA

**[DCA](/en/glossary/#dca)** (Dollar Cost Averaging) is a price-averaging strategy. Instead of buying all at once with your full amount, you buy equal portions at regular intervals.

Example:

- you decide to invest $1000 in bitcoin;
- instead of one purchase, you buy **$100 worth every week** for 10 weeks;
- when the price is low, you buy more satoshis;
- when the price is high, you buy fewer.

### Why DCA Works

DCA removes the beginner's biggest problem — **trying to time the perfect entry point** (market timing).

| Scenario | One-time purchase | DCA |
|-----------|---------------------|-----|
| Bought at the top | Loss, waiting for years | Averaged in, smaller loss |
| Bought at the bottom | Maximum profit | Profit, but smaller |
| Volatile market | Risk of bad entry | Smooth entry, minimal risk |

> DCA is **insurance against your own stupidity**. You can't predict the market — but you don't have to bet it all on one coin flip.

### How to Set Up DCA in Crypto

1. Decide how much you can invest monthly.
2. Pick an asset (BTC, ETH are the safest to start with).
3. Set up auto-buy on an exchange (many exchanges support this).
4. **Don't stare at the chart** between buys.
5. Repeat for at least 6–12 months.

## How to Read a Candlestick Chart

Even if you've chosen investing, understanding charts is useful. At least so you know the market's mood.

The most popular chart type is the **Japanese candlestick chart**.

### What a Candle Is Made Of

![Japanese candlestick structure](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/crypto-easy/crypto-easy.org/main/static/img/candlestick-basic.png)

Each candle shows price movement over a chosen time period (e.g., 1 hour, 1 day):

- **The body** — the difference between open and close price;
  - green (white) body — price went up;
  - red (black) body — price went down;
- **Upper wick** (shadow) — the highest price of the period;
- **Lower wick** (shadow) — the lowest price of the period.

### How to Read Trends

![Trend lines on TradingView](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/crypto-easy/crypto-easy.org/main/static/img/trend-lines.png)

- **Uptrend** — price makes higher highs and higher lows. Buy and hold.
- **Downtrend** — price makes lower highs and lower lows. Better to stay out.
- **Sideways (flat)** — price moves within a range. DCA works great — you buy at both the bottom and the top of the range.

> For a beginner, just one rule is enough: **if candles are green and climbing — the market is bullish. If they're red and falling — it's bearish.**

## Comparison: Investing vs Trading

| Criterion | Investing (HODL) | Trading |
|----------|----------------------|----------|
| Holding period | Months / years | Minutes / days / weeks |
| Time commitment | Low (an hour a month) | High (hours a day) |
| Stress | Low | High |
| Required knowledge | Basic | Deep (analysis, indicators) |
| Risk of losing your deposit | Moderate (market risk) | High (mistakes, leverage) |
| Returns | Historically 50–300% per cycle | Variable, usually losses |
| Taxes | Simpler (1–2 transactions) | Complex (hundreds of transactions) |
| Who it suits | Busy people, beginners | Professionals, full-time |

## Conclusion

Investing and trading are two different worlds. One is about patience and time. The other is about speed and nerves.

For a beginner, the choice is clear:

> **Start with investing. Use DCA. Don't try to beat the market.**

Trading is a job you need to show up prepared for — with experience, knowledge, and capital you can afford to lose.

For now, while you're just starting out — buy some bitcoin, turn off notifications, and come back in a year.

## FAQ

### Which is better: investing or trading?

For a beginner — investing, hands down. Trading requires experience, time, and psychological stamina. 80–90% of traders lose money.

### How much money do I need to start investing in crypto?

Any amount. You can start with $10–50 a month through DCA. Consistency matters more than size.

### Is DCA a guaranteed profit?

No. DCA reduces the risk of bad entry timing, but it doesn't protect against a market crash. If an asset drops 90% and never recovers, DCA won't save you. That's why you choose proven assets (BTC, ETH).

### Can I start trading from zero?

You can, but don't. First study the theory, open a demo account, practice with tiny amounts. And be prepared to lose money in your first few months — it's a normal part of the learning process.

### Which timeframe is best for a beginner?

For investing — daily (1D) or weekly (1W) candles. The higher the timeframe, the less noise and the less urge to make impulsive trades.
