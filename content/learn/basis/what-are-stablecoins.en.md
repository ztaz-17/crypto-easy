## Stablecoins: What Are USDT, USDC, and How They Work

---

You just bought your first Bitcoin. An hour later, the price drops 15%. You panic and open the chart — all red. Another day later — minus 30%. Sound familiar?

[Cryptocurrency](/en/glossary/#cryptocurrency) is a rollercoaster. Bitcoin can drop 50% in a week, and some altcoin can lose 90% in a day. Great for speculation. Terrible for savings.

Enter **[stablecoins](/en/glossary/#stablecoin)**.

> **A stablecoin** is a cryptocurrency whose price is pegged to something stable: a dollar, euro, or gold.

Buy USDT — and your money stops jumping. 1 USDT ≈ $1. Always.

In this article we'll cover:
- what [stablecoins](/en/glossary/#stablecoin) are and why they're needed;
- the three main stablecoins: **USDT**, **USDC**, and **DAI**;
- how they differ and which to choose;
- and why not all stablecoins are equally safe.

---

## Why stablecoins exist

### Crypto is too volatile

Bitcoin can rise 20% in a day — and drop 30% the next. ETH — same. Altcoins — worse. Keeping all your money in this "rollercoaster" is risky. Stablecoins solve this: the price stays put.

> **1 USDT = ~$1. Always.**

### Locking in profits

You bought Bitcoin at $30,000, it went to $70,000. You want to take profits but don't want to withdraw to a bank (slow, fees, taxes). Solution: sell Bitcoin for USDT. Profit is locked, money stays in crypto.

### Fast transfers

Need to send $10,000 abroad? Bank transfer — 3–5 days and 2–5% fee. USDT transfer via TRC-20 — 1 minute and $1–2 fee.

> **Stablecoins are money that moves at internet speed.**

### Bridge between fiat and crypto

Most exchanges don't trade Bitcoin directly for cash. The usual route:
1. Buy USDT via [P2P](/en/glossary/#p2p) or card.
2. Swap USDT for Bitcoin on a [CEX](/en/glossary/#cex) or [DEX](/en/glossary/#dex).

USDT is the universal "transit hub" between regular money and crypto.

## USDT (Tether)

### What it is

**USDT** (Tether) — the most popular stablecoin in the world. Market cap as of April 2026: $190 billion. It's on every exchange, every [wallet](/en/glossary/#wallet), every [DeFi](/en/glossary/#defi) protocol.

USDT runs on multiple blockchains: Ethereum (ERC-20), Tron (TRC-20 — most popular, low fees), BSC (BEP-20), Solana, Polygon, Avalanche, and more.

### How it works

Tether Limited — the company behind USDT — claims each [token](/en/glossary/#token) is backed by real dollars and assets. You give Tether $1, they mint 1 USDT. You return 1 USDT — they burn the token and give you $1 back.

> **Principle: 1 USDT = $1 in Tether's reserves.**

### The transparency problem

Tether has faced criticism. The company long withheld full audits. In 2021, Tether settled with the New York Attorney General for $18.5M — it emerged that at one point, reserves weren't fully backed.

Today the situation has improved: quarterly transparency reports, daily reserve attestations by BDO Italia, and a full audit underway by a Big Four firm (Deloitte, EY, KPMG, or PwC) in 2026.

> **USDT is a bet that Tether won't go bankrupt.**

For most traders, this risk is acceptable. But it's worth knowing about.

## USDC (USD Coin)

### What it is

**USDC** — the second most popular stablecoin. Issued by the Centre consortium, founded by Circle (a fintech company) and Coinbase (the largest US exchange). Market cap April 2026: $75 billion.

### How it works

Same principle: 1 USDC = $1 in reserves. But with a key difference:

> **USDC is regularly audited by Grant Thornton (a top-tier audit firm).**

Monthly reports. Circle shows that every USDC is backed by cash dollars or short-term US Treasury bonds — the safest assets.

### Why USDC is considered safer

- **Transparency.** Regular audits from a recognized firm.
- **Regulation.** Circle is a licensed financial company in the US.
- **Reserves.** Only dollars and US Treasuries — no risky assets.

Downside: USDC is less common on smaller exchanges and in some regions. On top exchanges (Binance, Bybit, OKX) — no issues.

## DAI (decentralized stablecoin)

### What it is

**DAI** — a stablecoin issued by no company. Its issuance is controlled by a smart contract on Ethereum. The protocol is called **MakerDAO**.

> **DAI — the first truly decentralized stablecoin.**

### How it works

Unlike USDT and USDC, DAI isn't backed by dollars in a bank. Instead:
1. A user deposits collateral — ETH, WBTC, USDC, or other assets — into the Maker smart contract.
2. The smart contract creates DAI against the collateral.
3. The loan must be maintained: if the collateral price drops, the smart contract auto-liquidates the position.

Example: you deposit $200 of ETH. The contract lets you create 150 DAI (collateral ratio ~133%). If ETH drops enough that collateral becomes insufficient — part of the collateral is sold to burn DAI and restore balance.

> **DAI holds its peg not through a company, but through algorithms and over-collateralization.**

### Pros

- **[Decentralization](/en/glossary/#decentralization).** Nobody can freeze your DAI or ban its issuance.
- **Transparency.** All transactions and collateral visible on the [blockchain](/en/glossary/#blockchain).
- **Independence.** No need to trust Tether or Circle — trust the code.

### Cons

- **Peg risk.** In March 2020, during a sharp ETH drop, DAI briefly de-pegged above $1 due to panic demand.
- **Complexity.** Harder for beginners than "buy USDT, get a dollar."
- **Ethereum dependency.** Network congestion means high fees for DAI creation and transfers.

## Warning: not all stablecoins are the same

### The Terra UST story — how NOT to build stablecoins

In 2022, **Terra UST** (TerraUSD) promised to hold $1 through an algorithm and its LUNA [token](/en/glossary/#token). No real backing — just math.

It worked... until it didn't. In May 2022, panic hit: large holders pulled their UST. The algorithm failed — UST crashed to $0. LUNA fell from $80 to $0.0002 in days.

> **Holder losses: $40 billion. One of the biggest catastrophes in crypto history.**

### Three types

- **Centralized (USDT, USDC).** Backed by real assets. Risk — the issuing company.
- **[Decentralized](/en/glossary/#decentralization) with collateral (DAI).** Backed by crypto. Risk — liquidations and de-pegging during crashes.
- **Algorithmic (Terra UST).** Backed by nothing. Only work while people believe. **Never use for savings.**

### Advice for beginners

> **Buy USDT or USDC. Store on major exchanges or trusted wallets. Don't chase "stablecoins with insane yields."**

## Conclusion

Stablecoins are the "safe harbor" of the crypto world. Lock in profits, send money abroad for pennies, and never worry about your savings dropping 50% overnight.

But stablecoins are not a "free dollar." Behind them are companies, algorithms, and risks:

- **USDT** — bet on Tether. Reliable for trading, transparency questions remain.
- **USDC** — bet on US regulation. More transparent, but less widespread.
- **DAI** — bet on [decentralization](/en/glossary/#decentralization). Works without companies, but more complex and riskier in crises.

Stablecoins aren't a 100% bank deposit replacement. But for crypto work — they're irreplaceable.

> **A stablecoin is a dollar that moves at internet speed.**

## FAQ

### Are stablecoins safe?

Safer than altcoins, less safe than physical cash or FDIC-insured US bank deposits. USDC is probably the safest stablecoin. But even it has risks.

### Can you lose money in a stablecoin?

Yes, several scenarios: issuer (Tether, Circle) goes bankrupt — USDT/USDC lose peg; algorithmic collapse (like Terra UST); smart contract hack (for DAI); technical error when transferring (wrong [blockchain](/en/glossary/#blockchain)); asset freeze by issuer (Circle can freeze USDC by regulatory order).

### Which stablecoin should a beginner choose?

**USDT** — for maximum exchange availability and low fees (via TRC-20).

**USDC** — if transparency matters and you use major exchanges or [DeFi](/en/glossary/#defi).

**DAI** — if you value decentralization and understand the topic.

> **Tip: don't keep all stablecoins in one place. Mix: some USDT for operations, some USDC for storage.**
