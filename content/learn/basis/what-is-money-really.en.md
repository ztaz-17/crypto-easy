## What Is Money Really (and Why It's Just Numbers)

---

Pull out your [wallet](/en/glossary/#wallet). Look at a banknote.

It's got a portrait, watermarks, a coat of arms, a serial number. It crinkles, you can fold it, tear it (though you probably shouldn't). From childhood we're taught that this is money. Something real. Tangible.

But what if I told you **that piece of paper is not money**?

It's just a physical representation of money. Like a gift certificate you can exchange for something else. And the real money we use every day… it doesn't exist in the physical world.

Sounds crazy? Let's break it down.

---

## Money as Records

### From gold to paper

A long time ago, money was gold. More precisely, **gold was money**. You had a gold coin — you owned value directly. Yuan, dollars, pounds were once just certificates: "the bearer of this receives this much gold."

You could exchange those certificates for real gold at a bank at any time. The paper was a convenient replacement — light, divisible, waterproof. But there was **real backing** behind it.

Then came 1971.

### Bretton Woods is dead. Long live the numbers!

In 1971, US President Richard Nixon announced that the dollar was no longer pegged to gold. It's called the "Nixon Shock." From that moment on, **no currency in the world has been backed by anything but trust**.

Paper money is now just paper.

But here's the paradox: we barely use paper. Open your banking app. What do you see? Numbers on a screen. Balance — $3,500. And where are those $3,500? In an ATM? Under your mattress?

> **90% of all money in the world is just numbers in databases.**

It has no physical form. There is no box of gold in the bank's vault that you can go and look at. There's only a record: "customer N owns 25,000 units of agreed-upon value."

### A simple experiment

Try sending a friend $50 from your card to theirs. What happened?

- You didn't pass a physical object
- You didn't hand over a bill
- Nothing moved through space

In Bank A's database, your record went down by $50. In Bank B's database, your friend's record went up by $50.

> **That's it. That's a money transfer. Just changing numbers in different databases.**

When you tap your card at a store — you're just giving a command to update a record. The terminal tells the bank: "deduct this much from that account and add this much to the store's account."

Money is **information**. Period.

## The Role of Banks

### A bank is not a vault

Most people think: "I put money in the bank, the bank keeps it safe, and when I need it, I take it out."

This is a **dangerous misconception**.

In reality, when you deposit money in a bank, you are **lending it to the bank**. In return, the bank promises to give it back on demand. Your balance is not money — it's the **bank's obligation** to repay you.

By law, banks don't have to keep all your money in a vault. They only need to hold a small fraction — usually 4–10% of all deposits. The rest they can use.

### Fractional reserve banking

This is called **[fractional reserve banking](/en/glossary/#fractional-reserve)**. Here's how it works:

1. You bring $100 to the bank and deposit it.
2. The bank must keep, say, $10 (10%) in reserve.
3. The remaining $90 is lent out to Peter.
4. Peter pays that $90 to a seller, who deposits it in her bank.
5. That bank keeps $9 in reserve and lends out $81…
6. …and so on.

Result: from the original $100, the money supply in the system has grown to roughly $900.

> **Banks don't just store money — they create it.**

That's why banks love lending and don't want you holding large sums in deposits. Every loan is new "numbers" in the system.

### Why this matters

Once you realize banks are not safes but factories that produce numbers, you start seeing the risks:

- Your money isn't "sitting" in the bank. It's "recorded" on a ledger.
- The bank can limit your access to it (freeze your card, [block](/en/glossary/#block) a transaction).
- If the bank has problems — anyone holding money above the insured amount risks losing it.
- You trust the bank, but the bank trusts your money to borrowers you never chose.

## Who Controls the System

### Central banks

Every country has a main bank — the **central bank** (People's Bank of China, US Federal Reserve, European Central Bank, Bank of Russia). It's not a commercial bank; it's a government body that:

- **Prints money** — decides how much new money enters the system.
- **Sets the key rate** — the interest rate at which it lends to commercial banks. The higher the rate, the more expensive loans are and the slower the economy. The lower it is, the cheaper money is and the higher [inflation](/en/glossary/#inflation) tends to be.
- **Regulates commercial banks** — issues and revokes licenses, audits reports, sets requirements.
- **Controls [inflation](/en/glossary/#inflation)** — tries to keep prices from rising too fast.

### Commercial banks

These are the banks where you have accounts. They're the executors:

- Open accounts
- Issue loans
- Process transfers
- Follow the rules set by the central bank

### Governments

Governments create the **rules of the game**: banking laws, currency regulations, tax codes. If the government decides the national currency is the only legal tender in the country, that's how it is. If it decides to impose restrictions on currency outflows — so be it.

> **The whole system is centralized and controlled from above.**

Someone decides how much money is in the economy. Someone decides who can open an account and who can't. Someone can freeze your account by court order — or even without one.

This isn't a "conspiracy." It's just the **architecture of the system**. And it's designed so that the final say always belongs to the regulator, not to you.

## Why It's All Just Trust

### What actually backs money?

There's no commodity behind the yuan. No oil behind the dollar. No gold behind the ruble.

> **There's only one thing behind money: a collective agreement among people to consider it valuable.**

This is called **[fiat money](/en/glossary/#fiat)** — from the Latin "fiat" (let it be so). Money exists because **everyone agreed** that it exists. The government says: "this is legal tender." People trust the government. And the system works.

### What if people stop believing?

A currency is worth exactly as much as people are willing to exchange for it in goods and services. If tomorrow everyone decides the currency is just paper, it becomes just paper.

This has happened before: hyperinflation in the Weimar Republic, in Zimbabwe, in Venezuela. Money that people stopped believing in was instantly devalued.

> **The financial system is a giant social construct held together by trust.**

### And what about cryptocurrency?

[Cryptocurrency](/en/glossary/#cryptocurrency) runs on the same principle — **trust**. But there's a key difference.

In the [fiat](/en/glossary/#fiat) system, you trust **people and organizations**: the central bank, the government, commercial banks. You trust they won't print too much money, freeze your account, or change the rules.

With [cryptocurrency](/en/glossary/#cryptocurrency), you trust **math**: open-source code, cryptography, network consensus. Nobody can "print" more bitcoin than the protocol allows. Nobody can freeze your funds if you hold your own [private keys](/en/glossary/#private-key).

Bitcoin is worth $X not because a president said so, but because millions of people around the world agree to buy and sell it at that price. **[Decentralized](/en/glossary/#decentralization) trust** instead of centralized trust.

## Conclusion

Let's sum it up:

1. **Money is records.** 90%+ of the world's money exists only as numbers in databases. Cash is just a physical interface to those records.
2. **Banks create money.** Through loans and fractional reserve banking, they multiply the money supply several times over.
3. **The system is centralized.** Central banks, governments, and commercial banks control who has access to how much "money."
4. **It all runs on trust.** Money is valuable only as long as we all agree it is.

When you look at your balance in the banking app, remember: you're looking at a number in someone else's database. That number is a promise. A promise that someone will give you something valuable in exchange for it.

Understanding that money is just numbers doesn't devalue it. On the contrary — it gives you clarity. You start seeing the system for what it is. And you can make conscious decisions: how to save, how to spend, how to protect your assets.

> **Money is a tool, not a value. A tool based on collective agreement.**

And like any tool, it's worth understanding — before you use it.

## FAQ

### If money is just records, can I "create" it?

Technically — no. You can't change a record in the bank's database. "Creating" money is the prerogative of banks through the lending mechanism. But you can create something that other people find valuable — and are willing to exchange money for. That's called "earning."

### What backs the dollar today?

Nothing physical. The dollar is a [fiat](/en/glossary/#fiat) currency backed only by trust in the US government and its economy. It used to be backed by gold, but since 1971 — it isn't. The same is true for the ruble, euro, yuan, and any other modern currency.

### Can a bank just vanish with my money?

Yes, it happens. In the US there's deposit insurance — $250,000 from the FDIC. In Russia, the Deposit Insurance Agency (DIA) covers up to 1.4 million rubles per depositor per bank. Many other countries have similar insurance schemes. The important thing to remember: insurance doesn't cover "all money" — just up to a limit. And it only kicks in if the bank loses its license, not in every situation where access is blocked.
