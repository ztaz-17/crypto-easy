# Why you're losing money even if you're saving

---

Imagine this: you're disciplined, setting money aside every month. You don't overspend, don't take out loans, keep a "financial safety cushion" in the bank.
The numbers in your account keep growing: **$10,000 → $20,000 → $30,000 ... $100,000**.

Seems like everything's going right.

But here's the paradox:
> **the more you save, the more you lose**.

Not because you're doing anything wrong.
But because the system itself is built so that your money slowly "burns" — silently, without notifications, without fees on your statement.

This isn't theft. It's worse — **savings erosion**.

Your deposit shows +5% per year.
But in reality, you're losing purchasing power — and every year you can buy less.

That's the big lie:

> **Growing numbers in your account ≠ growing wealth**

In this article we'll cover:
>- why deposits almost always lose to inflation;
>- how hidden inflation works;
>- how to calculate your **real return**;
>- and why "safe" money is often the most expensive.

Most importantly — we'll show you what alternatives exist and how to take back **control of your money**.

---

## Inflation vs. Your Deposit

### Official vs. Real inflation

When they say "inflation is 6%," it sounds tolerable.
Central banks have gotten virtuously good at juggling the numbers. Governments calculate inflation based on a consumer basket that includes hundreds of items:

* bread;
* gas;
* services;
* clothing.

But you don't live "by the basket" — that number has only a loose relationship with your actual life.

If you:
* rent a home;
* pay for healthcare;
* buy electronics;
* travel;

…your **personal inflation** might not be 6%, but 10–15%.

This is called **[inflation](/en/glossary/#inflation)** — and it can be hidden.

👉 Simple example:

> if spaghetti went up 50% and TVs went down 10%, the average inflation rate stays moderate.
> The problem is you eat spaghetti almost every day, but buy a TV once every five years.

Officially — inflation is low.
In reality — **your expenses went up 50–60%**.

---

### Why money "melts" even if you don't touch it

Money is not an asset.
It's just a **unit of account for value**.

When central banks print new money:
* the amount of money grows;
* there aren't more goods.

Result:
> **each unit of money is worth less**

Even if you leave your deposit untouched for 5 years —
its **[purchasing power](/en/glossary/#purchasing-power)** keeps falling.

That's **savings erosion**.

---

### Cash vs. Non-Cash: Which is worse?

Many people think:
* cash — bad (no return);
* deposit — good (pays interest).

But the reality is:

| Instrument | What happens |
| ---------- | ------------ |
| Cash | Loses to inflation |
| Deposit | Loses to inflation + gives small % |

Sounds strange, but:
> **the difference between cash and a deposit is (often) minimal**

Why?

Because the deposit rate:
* is below real inflation;
* is taxed;
* doesn't account for asset price growth (real estate, electronics).

---

### The mechanism of hidden erosion

Let's call things what they are. **Savings erosion** isn't when your money gets stolen. It's when it turns into a pumpkin, just sitting in a bank vault or a deposit account.
Imagine you bought a kilogram of gold, put it in a safe, and a year later opened it — and there was half a kilo.

> **Frustrating? Insanely so.**

But that's exactly what happens with fiat money, except we don't notice it because the bills look the same. What changes is what they're worth.

> **Key thought:** If your income or deposit returns are growing slower than gas and food prices, you're not "saving" money.
> You're financing whoever produces those goods, voluntarily giving up your share in the future.

---

### Comparison: How money ages at home vs. in the bank

Many people still debate: what's better — keeping cash under the mattress or putting it in the bank? Let's compare how fast $10,000 "melts" when left untouched.

| Year | Money at home (cash) at 15% inflation | Money in the bank (7% rate at 15% inflation) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Start** | $10,000 | $10,000 |
| **After 1 year** (purchasing power) | $10,000 − 15% = **$8,500** | $10,700 − 15% = **$9,095** |
| **After 5 years** (purchasing power) | $10,000 − (5 yrs × 15%) = **$4,437** | $13,500 − (5 yrs × 15%) = **$5,990** |

The difference between strategies is tiny. **Cash vs. non-cash** in this case is an argument about whether you burn for 7 minutes or 8.
In both cases, you lose in an economy where

> **the printing press runs faster than your labor.**

## Why "+5% annual return" is actually a loss

### Nominal vs. Real return

The bank shows:
> +5% per year

Sounds nice. But that's **nominal return**.

To get the truth, you need to calculate:
> **Real return = return − inflation**

Example:
* deposit: +5%
* inflation: 8%

Result:
> **real return = −3%**

You're losing. Every year.

---

### Deposit tax: the invisible minus

Even that +5% isn't fully yours.

In many countries:
* deposit interest is taxed (10–20%)

Example:
* rate: 5%
* tax: 15%

You get:
> 5% × (1 − 0.15) = **4.25%**

And now:
* inflation: 8%
* return: 4.25%

Real result:
> **−3.75% per year**

---

### Time value of money

There's another factor almost no one talks about:
> **money today is worth more than money tomorrow**

Why?
* you could have invested it;
* bought an asset;
* used an opportunity.

Holding money at a low rate means:
> **losing out on alternative profit**

---

### Real story

In 2018, you'd saved up $100,000 for a down payment on an apartment.

But you decided to "wait a couple of years" and put the money in a deposit at 6%.

After 5 years:
* your account grew to $130,000
* apartments nearly doubled in price

Now your money covers **a smaller share of the property than before**.

You didn't lose in numbers.

But you lost in reality.

## The simple math of loss

### The formula everyone should know

To figure out whether you're losing, use this simple principle:

> **Real return = (1 + deposit rate) ÷ (1 + inflation) − 1**

Where:
* deposit rate is the nominal percentage the bank promises;
* inflation is the real rise in prices for goods and services.

---

### A simple example

Let's say:
* deposit: 6%
* inflation: 10%

The math:
* (1.06 ÷ 1.10) − 1 ≈ −3.6%

Every year you lose **3.6% of your purchasing power**.

---

### What happens over 5–7 years

Now the magic of compound interest works against you.

If you're losing 3.6% per year:
* after 5 years → ~−18%
* after 7 years → ~−25%

> **A quarter of your money simply disappears**

No crises. No mistakes. Just "by the rules of the system."

---

### Real story #2

Maria was saving for her child's education.

7 years:
* she saved consistently;
* kept the money in a deposit.

Result:
* the amount grew by 30%;
* the cost of education went up by 80%.

She had to close the gap with a loan.

## Slow burn vs. alternatives

A deposit is like:
> holding ice in a warm room and being happy it's melting slowly

Cryptocurrency as a deposit alternative:
* gives access to yield (staking, DeFi);
* doesn't require intermediaries;
* gives you **control over your money**.

Yes, there are risks:
* volatility;
* user error;
* wrong asset choices.

But the key difference:
> **with crypto, you have a chance to outpace inflation**
> with a deposit — almost none

## Conclusion

If you're just saving — you're almost guaranteed to lose.

Not because you're bad at managing money.
But because:
* inflation eats purchasing power;
* deposits don't cover the losses;
* taxes reduce your return;
* time works against you.

> **Savings without a strategy aren't protection. They're an illusion of safety.**

Today it's not enough to just "save."

What matters is:
* understanding **real return**;
* accounting for inflation;
* finding tools that preserve capital.

## FAQ

### Is it true that keeping cash at home is better than a deposit?
Sometimes the difference is minimal. But cash loses to inflation faster because it doesn't even give you nominal returns.

### Can cryptocurrency protect against inflation if it's volatile?
Yes, but not any crypto and not always. In the long term, some crypto assets have shown growth above inflation, but they require understanding the risks.

### What is real return in simple terms?
It's how much you **actually earned**, accounting for price increases. If your money grew by 5% but prices went up by 10%, you're 5% poorer.

### What should I do if I'm afraid of risk?
Start by learning. Risk isn't the enemy. Not understanding — that's the enemy.
