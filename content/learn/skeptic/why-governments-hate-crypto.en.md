## Why Governments Hate Crypto (and Can't Ban It)

---

Imagine waking up tomorrow and discovering that the government **has lost control over money**.

People have stopped using banks. Nobody pays taxes — they're simply impossible to collect. Money flows abroad without authorities even knowing. The central bank prints rubles, dollars, or euros, but nobody accepts them.

Sounds like a dystopian scenario?

For governments — yes. That's exactly what they see in cryptocurrencies.

> **Crypto is a technology governments can't control — but also can't ban.**

And that puts them in an extremely uncomfortable position.

In this article we'll break down:
> - why governments are so nervous about crypto;
> - how they try to bring crypto under control;
> - and why a total ban is technically impossible.

---

## Control of Money Is the Foundation of State Power

### Why Money Control Matters to the State

Money isn't just paper or numbers on a screen. In the modern world, **money is a tool of governance**.

Through the monetary system, the government:

- collects taxes;
- regulates inflation;
- controls capital (what leaves the country and what stays);
- funds the budget;
- influences the economy through monetary policy.

If the government loses control over money, it loses the ability to govern.

> **Control over money issuance is one of the core functions of any government.**

That's why governments have fought so hard to make sure all payments go through banks — and that banks answer to central banks.

### Issuance, Taxes, Monetary Policy

The state has three key levers:

1. **Issuance** — who prints money and how much. Only the central bank can issue the national currency.
2. **Taxes** — the government decides how much you owe from what you earn.
3. **Monetary policy** — interest rates, reserves, inflation control.

Cryptocurrencies undermine **all three**:

- Bitcoin has a fixed supply that no central bank can change;
- crypto is hard to tax because of its pseudonymous nature;
- the government's monetary policy doesn't work if people stash their savings in crypto instead of the national currency.

### Crypto Undermines the Money Monopoly

Here's what really scares governments:

> **Cryptocurrency is money that doesn't need the state.**

It doesn't need banks, it doesn't need central banks, it doesn't need trust in the government. It runs on math and open-source code.

That's a direct threat to the state's monopoly on money — a monopoly that has existed for hundreds of years.

### Example: Bitcoin Bans in Some Countries

Governments' reactions are predictable.

**China** — in 2021 it completely banned all crypto operations, including mining. Exchanges were shut down, transactions were blocked.

**Nigeria** — in 2021 the central bank banned banks from serving crypto exchanges. Reason: massive flight to crypto due to naira inflation.

**Algeria, Nepal, Egypt** — full or partial bans on crypto.

But does it work? Spoiler: no. China banned it — and mining moved to the US, Kazakhstan, and Russia. P2P trading in China is still active.

## Taxes — The Government's Biggest Headache

### Why Crypto Is Hard to Tax

Taxes are what keeps the state running. Without taxes there's no budget, no army, no schools, no hospitals.

And this is where crypto creates a huge problem.

In the traditional system:

- every bank transfer is recorded;
- banks report to the tax authorities;
- hiding income is nearly impossible.

In crypto, it's completely different.

### Anonymity and Pseudonymity

Cryptocurrencies are **pseudonymous** by nature. You don't tie a wallet to your passport (unless you use centralized exchanges).

Yes, all transactions are recorded on the blockchain. But who stands behind a given address — nobody knows.

> **The government can't just show up at your door with a tax bill if it doesn't know you have crypto.**

Sure, blockchain analysis tools exist. But they only work if an address can be linked to a real identity — and that's not always possible.

### DeFi and the Difficulty of Tracking

A special headache for tax authorities comes from **DeFi** (decentralized finance):

- staking;
- lending and borrowing;
- yield farming;
- swaps through DEXs.

All of this happens without intermediaries. Without banks. Without reporting. The government simply doesn't see these transactions.

And if it doesn't see them — it can't tax them.

### How Governments Try to Regulate

This is where **exchanges** come into play. Since taxes are nearly impossible to collect via P2P and DeFi, governments put pressure on the one point of entry — centralized exchanges (CEXs).

Requirements for exchanges:

- mandatory **[KYC](/en/glossary/#kyc)** (identity verification);
- client reporting;
- freezing suspicious accounts;
- sharing data with tax authorities.

That's how the **IRS** works in the US (requires reports on every transaction), how regulators work in Europe (MiCA), and how they're trying to work in other countries.

But the problem is that crypto is global. When one exchange shuts down, users move to decentralized exchanges (DEXs) or P2P platforms.

## FATF and Global Recommendations

### What Is FATF

**FATF** (Financial Action Task Force) is an intergovernmental organization that sets standards for combating money laundering.

FATF recommendations aren't law — but countries that don't comply get put on the "grey list" and lose access to the international financial system.

> **All countries are forced to follow FATF rules if they want to work with global banks.**

### Travel Rule

One of FATF's key requirements for the crypto industry is the **Travel Rule**.

It requires:

- service providers (exchanges, swap services) to share sender and recipient information with each other;
- if you send crypto from Exchange A to Exchange B — both exchanges must exchange your personal data.

The Travel Rule effectively kills anonymity on centralized platforms.

### Recommendations for All Countries

FATF recommends that countries:

- license crypto exchanges and service providers;
- require them to comply with **[AML](/en/glossary/#aml)** and **[KYC](/en/glossary/#kyc)**;
- monitor suspicious transactions;
- penalize violations.

Most developed countries have adopted these recommendations.

### How This Affects the Crypto Industry

On one hand — regulation legitimizes crypto, makes it more "official."

On the other — regulation kills the very idea of decentralization. If every exchange is forced to collect your data and hand it over to the government — how is that different from a bank?

### Exchange Licensing

Today, getting a crypto exchange license is expensive and difficult:

- a license in **Singapore** requires millions in reserves;
- **MiCA** in Europe requires registration in every country;
- in the **US** — patchwork regulation at the state level (BitLicense in New York).

As a result, small players leave, and only the big ones that can afford compliance remain.

## Restrictions vs Total Bans

### Which Countries Have Banned Crypto

According to research, around 50 countries have introduced full or partial crypto bans.

Total ban (using crypto is illegal):

- **China** — operations, mining, and exchanges prohibited;
- **Algeria** — criminal liability;
- **Nepal** — all operations prohibited;
- **Egypt** — banned on both religious and legal grounds.

Partial restrictions:

- **India** — inconsistent policy, bans and permissions come and go;
- **Turkey** — crypto payments prohibited, but trading is allowed;
- **Bangladesh** — illegal, but not actively prosecuted.

### Which Countries Regulate

Most major economies have chosen the path of regulation rather than a ban:

- **US** — regulated through the SEC, CFTC, and IRS;
- **EU** — adopted MiCA (unified regulation across all member states);
- **UAE** — created free zones for crypto companies;
- **Singapore** — one of the most advanced regulatory frameworks;
- **Switzerland** — "Crypto Valley" in Zug;
- **El Salvador** — made Bitcoin legal tender.

### Why a Total Ban Doesn't Work

Banning crypto is kind of like banning the internet. You can try technically, but practically — it's impossible.

Reasons:

1. Crypto has no central server. You can't just "turn it off."
2. The blockchain runs on thousands of nodes around the world.
3. P2P exchange doesn't require intermediaries.

### Example: China

China is the most telling example. In 2021 the country:
- banned all crypto operations;
- shut down exchanges;
- banned mining.

What actually happened?

- Miners moved to the US, Kazakhstan, Russia. Bitcoin's network hashrate barely changed.
- P2P trading survived — Chinese people trade USDT through WeChat and Alipay.
- Crypto simply moved into the "grey zone."

> **The ban didn't destroy crypto. It just pushed it outside the legal framework.**

### Decentralization Makes Bans Technically Impossible

Here's the key point many people miss:

> **Cryptocurrency is not a company, not a server, not a country. It's a protocol.**

Bitcoin belongs to nobody. Ethereum belongs to nobody. They exist as long as at least one node in the network exists.

To ban Bitcoin, you'd need to:
- shut down the internet in the country (which has been tried — and it doesn't work);
- physically ban all P2P transfers;
- control every computer and phone.

No government is capable of that.

## Why Governments Can't Ban Crypto Entirely

### No Central Point to Shut Down

Bitcoin, Ethereum, Monero, and other decentralized networks have no server. They aren't hosted in a single data center. There's no "red button" you can press to turn everything off.

Every node is an independent network participant. To kill the network, you'd have to destroy most of the nodes — and they're distributed all over the world.

### P2P Can't Be Blocked

Even if a government bans exchanges, people can trade crypto directly:

- through P2P platforms (LocalBitcoins, Paxful);
- through messaging apps (Telegram, WhatsApp);
- in person, with cash.

Blocking all these channels is physically impossible.

### Nodes Around the World

At the time of writing, Bitcoin's network has tens of thousands of nodes in hundreds of countries. To block the network, all those countries would have to simultaneously ban running nodes — which is highly unlikely.

### Crypto Is a Protocol, Like the Internet

The most accurate comparison:

> **Cryptocurrency is a protocol — like TCP/IP, HTTP, or Tor.**

You can't "ban" TCP/IP. You can't "ban" HTTP. You can ban individual websites or services, but not the protocol itself.

Same with crypto. You can ban exchanges, ban mining, ban advertising. But the blockchain keeps running.

### Comparison: Banning Crypto = Banning the Internet

Think about it:

- Can you completely ban the internet in one country?
- Yes, but at what cost? North Korea is an example. Total control, isolation, no economic development.

- Can you ban the internet worldwide?
- Technically impossible.

> **Cryptocurrency is the internet for money. You can't ban it any more than you can ban the internet.**

## Conclusion

Governments don't like crypto. And that's normal.

Cryptocurrency threatens:
- the monopoly on money issuance;
- the tax control system;
- the ability to manage capital;
- the traditional financial system.

That's why governments try to regulate, restrict, and sometimes ban crypto.

But there's a fundamental problem:

> **Cryptocurrency is a technology — and you can't ban technology.**

You can ban exchanges — but not P2P.
You can ban mining — but not the network.
You can block websites — but not the protocol.

Crypto will exist as long as the internet exists. And governments will have to come to terms with that.

The question isn't "will they ban crypto?"
The question is **how will governments learn to coexist with it**.

The outcome is clear: regulation is inevitable. But a total ban — no.

> **Crypto is freedom that can't be taken away. Even if they really want to.**

## FAQ

### Which countries have fully banned crypto?
Total bans are in effect in China, Algeria, Nepal, Egypt, and Bangladesh (partial). In total, around 50 countries have introduced some kind of restriction. But even in these countries, crypto continues to be used unofficially.

### Can a government block Bitcoin?
Technically — no. Bitcoin has no central server. To block the network, you'd have to shut down every node in the world — which is impossible. A government can only restrict access to exchanges and services within its own territory.

### What is the FATF Travel Rule?
The Travel Rule is a FATF requirement that obligates crypto service providers (exchanges, swap services) to share sender and recipient information with each other. Effectively — the end of anonymity on centralized platforms.

### Do I have to pay taxes on crypto?
In most countries — yes. Cryptocurrency is considered property or an asset, and profit from selling it is taxable. Specific rules depend on the country. Tax evasion can lead to serious consequences, including criminal liability.
