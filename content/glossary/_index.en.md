## Crypto Glossary

Understand key cryptocurrency and blockchain terms in plain language.

---

### Airdrop

Free distribution of tokens to new users to promote a project. Often used to attract attention to new protocols.

---

### AML

*Anti-Money Laundering* — a set of rules requiring exchanges and banks to verify the origin of funds and report suspicious transactions.

---

### APY / APR

Annual percentage yield. APY (Annual Percentage Yield) accounts for compound interest (capitalization), APR (Annual Percentage Rate) does not. In DeFi, APY is more commonly used since interest accrues continuously.

---

### Bag

Slang term for a portfolio of cryptocurrency that a trader holds hoping for a price increase. "I have a big bag of ETH."

---

### Bear / Bull

**Bear Market** — prolonged price decline, trader pessimism. **Bull Market** — sustained price growth, optimism and euphoria. These terms come from traditional financial markets.

---

### BEP-20

Token standard on the BNB Chain (formerly Binance Smart Chain). Fully compatible with ERC-20, making it easy to transfer tokens between Ethereum and BSC. Fees on BSC are significantly lower than Ethereum. Addresses start with `0x`.

---

### Block

The structural unit of a blockchain, containing confirmed transactions. Each new block contains a reference (hash) to the previous one, forming a chain — hence the name "blockchain."

---

### Blockchain

A chain of blocks — a distributed database where each block contains transaction records and a reference to the previous block. Data cannot be changed retroactively. The foundation of Bitcoin, Ethereum, and other cryptocurrencies.

---

### Bridge

A service for transferring assets between different blockchains (e.g., from Ethereum to Arbitrum). Allows you to use the same tokens across different networks, but be careful: bridges are often hacked.

---

### CEX

*Centralized Exchange* — a centralized exchange (Binance, Bybit, Coinbase). Works like a traditional platform: you register, complete KYC, and the exchange holds your funds.

---

### Cold Wallet

A wallet that is **not connected to the internet**. For example, hardware (Ledger, Trezor) or paper. Used for long-term storage of large amounts — hackers cannot access it remotely. Opposite of hot wallet.

---

### Cryptocurrency

Digital currency secured by cryptography. Runs on blockchain technology. Does not require intermediaries (banks) for transfers between people.

---

### Custodial Service

A service that stores your private keys and controls your funds (e.g., an exchange). You trust your money to a third party. Opposite of a non-custodial wallet.

---

### DAI

A decentralized stablecoin issued by the MakerDAO protocol on Ethereum. Backed by over-collateralized crypto assets (ETH, WBTC, USDC). The peg is maintained algorithmically through smart contracts, not by a centralized company. 1 DAI ≈ $1.

---

### DAO

*Decentralized Autonomous Organization* — an organization governed by code (smart contracts) and token holder voting rather than a hierarchy of managers. Examples: MakerDAO, Uniswap DAO.

---

### DApp

*Decentralized Application* — an application that runs on a blockchain through smart contracts. No one can shut it down or change the rules unilaterally. Examples: Uniswap (exchange), Aave (lending), OpenSea (NFTs).

---

### DCA

*Dollar Cost Averaging* — a strategy of buying crypto for a fixed amount at regular intervals, regardless of price. Reduces the risk of buying at the peak.

---

### Decentralization

The absence of a central control point. In crypto, this means the network runs on thousands of independent computers (nodes), and no one can unilaterally shut it down or change the rules.

---

### DeFi

*Decentralized Finance* — an ecosystem of financial services (lending, exchange, insurance) on blockchain, operating through smart contracts without intermediaries. Examples: Uniswap, Aave, MakerDAO.

---

### DEX

*Decentralized Exchange* — a decentralized exchange. Crypto trading happens directly through smart contracts, without an intermediary. No registration or KYC required. Examples: Uniswap, PancakeSwap, Curve.

---

### DYOR

*Do Your Own Research* — crypto is full of scammers and hype, so don't take things at face value — always check the project, team, code, and tokenomics before investing.

---

### ERC-20

Token standard on the Ethereum network. Defines basic functions for transfers, balance checks, and spending approvals. Most tokens on Ethereum (USDT, USDC, UNI) use ERC-20. Other networks (BSC, Polygon) have compatible standards.

---

### Exit Liquidity

Someone who buys an asset at its peak, allowing early investors to exit with profit. In the world of memecoins and hype projects, retail buyers often become exit liquidity for large players.

---

### Fiat

Fiat money — traditional currencies issued by governments (USD, EUR, RUB). Backed only by trust in the government, not by gold or other assets.

---

### FOMO

*Fear Of Missing Out* — the emotion that drives people to buy crypto at the peak because "everyone is buying and I might miss out."

---

### Fork

A protocol change where the chain splits into two. **Hard forks** are incompatible changes (new coin), **soft forks** are backward-compatible. Example: Bitcoin Cash is a hard fork of Bitcoin.

---

### Fractional Reserve

A banking system where the bank keeps only a small fraction of deposits (typically 5-10%) and lends out the rest. This creates "virtual" money.

---

### FUD

*Fear, Uncertainty, Doubt* — negative news and rumors spread to create panic and make people sell their assets. Often used as a weapon against competitors.

---

### Gas

The fee for executing an operation on the Ethereum network (and other EVM networks). Measured in gwei (1 gwei = 0.000000001 ETH). Higher gas means faster transaction processing.

---

### Hash Function

A mathematical algorithm that converts any data into a fixed-length string (hash). The smallest change in input completely changes the hash. The foundation of blockchain security.

---

### HODL

*Hold On for Dear Life* — holding cryptocurrency for a long time without selling, regardless of price fluctuations. The term originated from a typo of "hold" on a Bitcoin forum in 2013.

---

### Hot Wallet

A wallet connected to the internet (MetaMask, Trust Wallet). Convenient for daily transactions but less secure than a cold wallet. Suitable for small amounts.

---

### Impermanent Loss

The loss you incur when providing liquidity to a pool (e.g., on Uniswap) if the token prices change relative to when you entered. The larger the price change, the greater the loss.

---

### Inflation

The devaluation of money over time. When central banks print new money, each existing unit becomes worth less. In crypto with limited supply (Bitcoin), inflation is absent by design.

---

### KYC

*Know Your Customer* — identity verification on exchanges. Requires submitting a passport, selfie, and sometimes proof of address. A mandatory regulatory requirement in most countries.

---

### L1

*Layer 1* — the base blockchain that provides security and network consensus. Examples: Bitcoin, Ethereum, Solana, BNB Chain. L2 solutions are built on top of L1 for scaling.

---

### L2 (Layer 2)

A second-layer solution built on top of a base blockchain (L1). Allows faster and cheaper transaction processing while leveraging the base layer's security. Examples: Arbitrum, Optimism, Lightning Network, Polygon.

---

### Lightning Network

A protocol on top of the Bitcoin blockchain for instant, low-cost payments. Allows opening payment channels between participants without recording every transaction on the main chain.

---

### Liquidity

The ability of an asset to be quickly sold at market price without significant loss of value. The higher the liquidity of a pair, the lower the slippage during trading.

---

### Meme Coin

A cryptocurrency with no serious utility, based on memes, hype, and community. Prices are extremely volatile. Examples: Dogecoin, Shiba Inu, PEPE. Pure speculation — be careful.

---

### Network Fee

The fee paid to miners or validators for processing a transaction. Does not depend on the transfer amount, but on network congestion. Can sometimes exceed the transfer amount.

---

### NFT

*Non-Fungible Token* — a unique digital asset that cannot be replaced by another identical one. Used for digital art, tickets, gaming, domain names. Standards: ERC-721, ERC-1155.

---

### NGMI

*Not Gonna Make It* — slang meaning "won't succeed," "won't take off." Used in crypto circles to describe a project, strategy, or person with no future in the industry.

---

### Non-Custodial Wallet

A wallet where you control your private keys and seed phrase. No one can freeze your funds. Examples: MetaMask, Trust Wallet, Ledger.

---

### Nonce

A transaction number in a wallet that increments by 1 with each new outgoing transaction. Starts at 0. If you sent a transaction with nonce=5, the next one will be nonce=6. Helps prevent double-spending.

---

### P2P

*Peer-to-Peer* — direct exchange between people without an intermediary. A P2P exchange (e.g., Binance P2P) lets you buy crypto from another person directly. You negotiate the price and payment method yourself.

---

### Private Key

A secret code that gives access to your crypto funds. Whoever holds the private key owns the coins. Never share it with anyone.

---

### Proof of Stake

*PoS* — a consensus algorithm where validators are chosen based on the amount of coins they have staked (locked up). Used by Ethereum after The Merge. More energy-efficient than PoW.

---

### Proof of Work

*PoW* — a consensus algorithm where miners solve complex mathematical problems to add a block. Used by Bitcoin. Requires significant electricity but provides maximum security.

---

### Public Address

A string of characters you can give to others to receive crypto. Like a bank account number. Example: `0x1a2B...3c4D`. You can share your address publicly, but never your private key.

---

### Pump and Dump

A fraudulent scheme: a group of traders artificially inflate a coin's price (pump), attracting retail buyers with hype, then sell off en masse (dump), crashing the price. Regular investors are left with losses.

---

### Purchasing Power

The quantity of goods and services that can be bought with a certain amount of money. Decreases with inflation: $100 today buys less than it did a year ago.

---

### Rekt

Slang from "wrecked" — destroyed. Means a trader lost all or most of their money on a bad trade, liquidation, or scam. "I bought SHIB at the top — totally rekt."

---

### Revoke

Canceling a permission you previously gave to a smart contract to access your tokens. If you approved (approve) a malicious contract, revoking can save your funds. Use services like Revoke.cash.

---

### Rug Pull

A type of scam where project creators drain all liquidity from the pool and disappear with investors' money. One of the most common schemes in DeFi and memecoins.

---

### Seed Phrase

12 or 24 English words that restore access to your wallet. This is the master key to all your funds. Write it on paper, store it safely. Never share it.

---

### SegWit

*Segregated Witness* — a Bitcoin protocol upgrade that separates transaction signatures from main data. Increases block capacity and reduces fees. SegWit addresses start with `bc1`.

---

### Slippage

The difference between the expected trade price and the actual execution price. Caused by low liquidity or high volatility. In DeFi, you can manually set the acceptable slippage percentage.

---

### Staking

Locking up coins in a network to support its operation (validating transactions) and earn income. Works on the Proof of Stake principle. Rewards are usually paid in the same coin you staked.

---

### Tokenomics

The model of token distribution, issuance, and utility. Shows how many tokens the team, investors, and community hold, how they unlock, and why the token exists. The most important factor for analyzing any project.

---

### TRC-20

Token standard on the TRON (TRX) network. Similar to ERC-20 on Ethereum. Widely used for USDT: fees on TRON are significantly lower than on Ethereum. Addresses start with `T`.

---

### TVL

*Total Value Locked* — the total value of all funds locked in a DeFi protocol's smart contracts. Measured in USD. Higher TVL means more trust in the protocol and lower scam risk.

---

### USDC

USD Coin — a stablecoin pegged 1:1 to the US dollar. Issued by Circle in partnership with Coinbase. Reserves are regularly audited. Considered one of the most reliable stablecoins.

---

### USDT

Tether — the largest stablecoin, pegged 1:1 to the US dollar. Used on all exchanges and in DeFi. USDT trading volume exceeds Bitcoin's. Criticized for opaque reserves.

---

### Wallet

A crypto wallet — a program (MetaMask, Trust Wallet) or device (Ledger, Trezor) for storing private keys and sending transactions. A wallet doesn't physically hold coins — it gives you access to them on the blockchain.

---

### Wen Moon

A joking question from crypto slang: "When moon?" meaning when will the coin's price skyrocket. Used in chats and social media as a meme asking "when will we get rich?"

---

### Whale

A large holder of cryptocurrency with enough coins to influence the price through their actions. Whales can manipulate the market: dump by selling or pump by buying large amounts.

---

### Whitelist

A list of wallet addresses approved for early token sales (or minting) before public access. Getting on a whitelist often requires completing tasks or participating in the project's community.

---

### Whitepaper

A project document describing the technology, tokenomics, roadmap, and team. Always read the whitepaper before investing — if it doesn't exist or looks copied, that's a red flag.
