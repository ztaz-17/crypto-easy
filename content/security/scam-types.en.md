## Common Types of Crypto Scams

---

Let's be real — crypto attracts scammers like honey attracts bees.

Anonymous transactions, irreversible transfers, and the fact that there's no "customer support hotline that will get your money back" make crypto an ideal target for bad actors.

> **Nobody will return your stolen crypto.**

Not the bank. Not the police. Not the [blockchain](/en/glossary/#blockchain) creators.

That's why every user needs to know how to spot threats themselves.

In this article we'll cover:

>- the most common types of crypto scams;
>- how to recognize them;
>- what to do to avoid getting caught.

---

## Phishing: Theft Through Impersonation

### How It Works

**Phishing** is the most widespread type of scam. Scammers create fake websites, apps, or messages that look exactly like the real deal.

The goal — trick you into entering:

- your [seed phrase](/en/glossary/#seed-phrase);
- your [private key](/en/glossary/#private-key);
- your exchange password.

The moment you do — your funds are gone in seconds.

### Real-World Examples

- **Fake MetaMask** — a site like `metamask-login.com` asks you to "verify your wallet";
- **Official-looking email from "Binance"** — "Your account has been locked, click here";
- **Text from "the exchange"** — the link leads to a fake login page;
- **Google ads** — the top search result for "Uniswap" can be a clone.

### How to Protect Yourself

- always check the URL — the real address must be exact, no variations;
- use bookmarks, not search results or email links;
- never enter your seed phrase on any website — even if the design looks legit;
- install a phishing-blocker extension like Wallet Guard.

> **A real service will never ask for your seed phrase.**

## Fake Websites: One Character Off and You're Done

### How It Works

Scammers register domains that look visually identical to the real ones.

Examples:

- `binance.com` → `binance.org`, `binance-secure.com`, `b1nance.com`;
- `opensea.io` → `opensea.xyz`, `opensea-nft.net`;
- `etherscan.io` → `etherscan.pro`, `etherscan.live`.

You land on one of these through an ad, an email, or a social media message. Then it's either phishing or connecting your wallet and signing a malicious transaction.

### How to Protect Yourself

- check the domain **before** entering any data;
- save important sites as bookmarks;
- don't click links from untrusted sources;
- use DNS filters or anti-phishing browser extensions.

## Airdrop Traps: "Free Tokens" That Steal

### How It Works

An [airdrop](/en/glossary/#airdrop) is a free token giveaway meant to attract users. Scammers abuse this mechanism:

1. They promise free tokens — you just need to "connect your wallet."
2. You go to the site and hit "Claim."
3. The site asks you to sign a transaction or "verify your address."
4. In reality you're signing approval to transfer all your tokens away.

### Real-World Examples

- **Uniswap UNI airdrop** — dozens of fake sites popped up after the real Uniswap airdrop;
- **Fake NFT mints** — "free [NFT](/en/glossary/#nft) mint" that drains your ETH when you sign;
- **Token approval scam** — the site asks you to sign an `approve` for an unlimited amount.

### How to Protect Yourself

- never sign a transaction you don't fully understand;
- check what you're actually signing: amount, contract address, fee;
- if an airdrop promises too much — it's a scam;
- use transaction simulation extensions like Blowfish or Pocket Universe;
- real airdrops never ask you to "pay for gas by connecting your wallet."

> **There's no such thing as a free lunch.**

## Fake Support on Telegram and Discord

### How It Works

Scammers create fake "support" accounts for exchanges, wallets, and [DeFi](/en/glossary/#defi) protocols. They find users on Telegram, Discord, or Twitter (X) and offer help.

The script:

1. You post a complaint in a public chat.
2. Instantly you get a DM: "Message me privately, I'm from support."
3. The "agent" asks you to "verify your wallet," "go through validation," or "send a test transaction."
4. One ending — your money is gone.

### How to Spot It

- real support **never messages you first**;
- real support never asks for your seed phrase, private key, or password;
- official accounts have a verification checkmark;
- support links should only come from the official website.

### How to Protect Yourself

- ignore all DMs from "support";
- on Telegram, adjust your privacy settings to block messages from strangers;
- contact support **only through official channels** (the Help page on the website);
- use official bots (e.g., @BinanceHelpBot) and verify them through the website.

## Screen Sharers: "Let Me Help You Set Up Your Wallet"

### How It Works

**Screen sharers** — scammers who ask you to share your screen under the pretense of "helping with setup."

It goes like this:

1. Someone offers help over Telegram, Zoom, or AnyDesk.
2. They ask you to share your screen.
3. While you watch, they record your seed phrase, private keys, or passwords.
4. Sometimes they ask you to go to a site and sign a transaction — "it's safe, don't worry."

### Real-World Examples

- **"Let me help you set up MetaMask"** — they ask to see your screen to "check the settings";
- **"Ledger tech support"** — they ask you to install AnyDesk and share your screen;
- **"Wallet security check"** — they ask you to open your wallet and "confirm your address."

### How to Protect Yourself

- **never** share your screen with strangers;
- don't install remote access software (AnyDesk, TeamViewer) at the request of "helpers";
- if you need help, ask trusted friends or consult official sources;
- store your seed phrase on paper only — never type it on a computer while screen sharing is active.

## Other Common Scams

### Rug Pull

Project creators collect investor money, then disappear with it.

Red flags:

- anonymous team;
- aggressive marketing promising "guaranteed returns";
- contract code hasn't been audited.

### Pump & Dump

A group of traders:

- buys up an obscure token;
- hypes it up through promotional channels (pump);
- sells at the peak (dump) — everyone else loses money.

### Fake Exchange

The site looks like a real exchange. You deposit funds, see your balance grow, but can't withdraw — it's all fake.

Red flags:

- no verification through reputable services;
- promises "deposit bonuses";
- suspiciously low fees.

### Romance Scam / Pig Butchering

A scammer builds trust through social media or dating apps, suggests "investing in crypto," then vanishes with the money.

## Quick Reference: Scam Type → How to Spot → What to Do

- **Phishing**: URL differs by one character; asked to enter seed phrase → Check the URL manually; don't enter your data
- **Fake Website**: Domain uses `.org`, `.pro`, `.live` suffixes → Use bookmarks; check the SSL certificate
- **Airdrop Trap**: Promises "free money"; asks you to sign a transaction → Verify the contract on Etherscan; simulate the transaction
- **Fake Support**: Messages you first; asks for keys or payments → Ignore; only contact support from the official site
- **Screen Sharer**: Asks to view your screen or install AnyDesk → Don't share your screen; don't install remote access
- **Rug Pull**: Anonymous team; no audit; promises "guaranteed returns" → Check the team; look for audits from reputable firms
- **Pump & Dump**: Aggressive social media marketing; sudden volume spike → Don't buy into hype; check the token's history
- **Fake Exchange**: No licenses; weird bonuses; can't withdraw → Check license registries; search for real user reviews

## How to Verify a Link: Step-by-Step Checklist

Before clicking any link or entering your data, run through these 5 steps:

1. **Look at the URL** — is there a typo or a strange domain?
2. **Check the SSL** — is there a padlock and a valid certificate?
3. **Compare with the official source** — open the site via your bookmark, not the link.
4. **Check the domain registration date** via Whois — fresh domains (under 6 months) are suspicious.
5. **Search the name + "scam"** — if scammers have already been active there, you'll find warnings.

## General Safety Rules

- **Never share your seed phrase.** Not with "support," not even with family.
- **Don't sign blindly.** Every transaction is an action with real money.
- **Don't click links from social media** — scammers impersonate official project accounts.
- **Keep most of your funds on a cold wallet.** If you're not touching the money, let it sit on a Ledger or Trezor.
- **Use a separate wallet for experiments.** Only sign suspicious transactions on a wallet with a minimal balance.
- **Enable two-factor authentication (2FA)** on exchanges — use Google Authenticator, not SMS.

---

## What to Do If You've Already Been Scammed

If you've sent crypto to scammers:

1. **Accept it** — blockchain transactions are irreversible. Getting funds back is nearly impossible.
2. **Lock down access** — if your seed phrase was compromised, immediately move any remaining funds to a new wallet.
3. **Warn the community** — post about it on social media. Scammers reuse the same addresses.
4. **Contact the exchange's security team** — if you sent funds to an exchange (e.g., Binance), they may freeze the scammer's account, though this is rare.
5. **File a police report** — the chance of recovering your money is minimal, but for large sums it may help freeze the scammer's accounts.

## Conclusion

Crypto is the technology of the future. But with freedom comes responsibility.

> **In crypto, there's no "bank" that will return stolen funds. No insurance. No "undo transfer" button.**

Your only defense is your own vigilance.

The golden rules:

- don't trust strangers;
- don't enter data on suspicious sites;
- don't sign transactions you don't understand;
- use [cold wallets](/en/glossary/#cold-wallet) for storage;
- always verify information from multiple sources.

Crypto scammers only exist because their schemes still work. Don't give them a chance.

## FAQ

### How can I tell if a website is fake?
Check the URL, the domain registration date (Whois), look for typos and missing HTTPS. When in doubt — open the official site from your bookmarks.

### My wallet was hacked — can I get my money back?
Almost never. Blockchain transactions are irreversible. The only exception is if you sent to an exchange — the exchange admins may freeze the scammer's account, but this is rare.

### What if I accidentally signed a dangerous transaction?
1. Immediately revoke the approval on Revoke.cash or Etherscan.
2. Transfer any remaining funds to a new wallet.
3. From now on, always simulate transactions before signing.

### How do I tell a real airdrop from a scam?
Real airdrops:
- are announced on the project's official channels;
- don't ask you to sign suspicious transactions;
- don't require paying "gas" through third-party sites.

Fake airdrops:
- promise "huge amounts for free";
- ask you to connect your wallet on an untrusted site;
- demand that you sign an `approve` for an unlimited amount.

### Should I use anti-phishing browser extensions?
Yes. Wallet Guard, Pocket Universe, and Blowfish are great tools. They warn you about dangerous transactions and suspicious sites. But don't rely on them alone — basic safety rules matter more.
