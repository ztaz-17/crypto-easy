## What Are NFTs and Why They Matter (Beyond Pictures)

---

You've probably heard this story: in 2021, a digital image — a collage of 5,000 pictures — sold for **$69 million**. The buyer didn't get a physical canvas; they got a file and a record on the [blockchain](/en/glossary/#blockchain). The world gasped: "Why?"

That was an **NFT** — a non-fungible token.

But reducing [NFTs](/en/glossary/#nft) to just expensive pictures is like reducing the internet to email. Let's break down what this technology actually is and where it's already being used.

---

## What Is an NFT

### Fungible vs Non-Fungible

In everyday life, we constantly deal with **fungible** things:

- one dollar equals another dollar;
- one bitcoin equals another bitcoin;
- one bag of sugar in a store is the same as any other.

These assets can be swapped around without losing value.

**Non-fungible** things are unique:

- a concert ticket — each one has its own seat and row;
- an apartment in a building — each has its own number and layout;
- an artist's signature on a painting — one of a kind.

NFTs are the digital equivalent of a "unique item."

---

### How It Works

An NFT is a **token** (a record on the blockchain) that:

- stores metadata (a link to the file, description, attributes);
- tracks ownership history;
- **cannot be split** into smaller pieces;
- **is not interchangeable** with another token.

Standards:

- **ERC-721** (Ethereum) — the most popular;
- **ERC-1155** — lets you create both unique and fungible tokens in a single contract.

👉 Important: the actual file (image, music, video) is usually not stored on the blockchain itself, but in a decentralized storage system (IPFS) or on a regular server. The blockchain only holds a link.

## Beyond Pictures: What Else NFTs Are Used For

The media hype around NFTs in 2021–2022 was mostly about digital art. But the real-world applications go much further.

---

### 1. Event Tickets

Some concert venues and sports clubs already use NFT tickets.

Benefits:

- **authenticity** — blockchain records can't be forged;
- **secondary market** — resales are controlled, and organizers can earn royalties;
- **keepsake** — the ticket stays in your wallet as a souvenir with history.

Example: European football clubs like FC Barcelona issue NFT tickets for matches.

---

### 2. Gaming (In-Game Items)

Players have spent real money on skins, weapons, and characters for years — but all of it lives on the developer's servers. If the server shuts down, the items disappear.

NFTs in games give you:

- **true ownership** — the item belongs to you, not the developer;
- **portability** — in theory, a CoD skin could be used in another game (if the developers agree);
- **trading** — you can sell an item on a marketplace without the game creator's permission.

Examples: Gods Unchained (a collectible card game), Decentraland (a virtual world).

---

### 3. Membership and Access

An NFT can work as a **digital pass**:

- access to a private club or chat;
- discounts with project partners;
- voting rights in a [DAO](/en/glossary/#dao) (decentralized autonomous organization).

Example: Bored Ape Yacht Club — NFT holders get access to exclusive events, merchandise, and community.

---

### 4. Documents and Certificates

Diplomas, driver's licenses, course completion certificates — all of these can be issued as NFTs.

Why it's convenient:

- authenticity can be verified with one click (no need to dig through archives);
- the document can't be forged;
- you carry it in your wallet instead of keeping a paper copy.

Some universities have already started issuing diplomas on the blockchain.

---

### 5. Domains and Identifiers

NFT domains (e.g., `yourname.crypto`) aren't DNS addresses — they're **wallet names**. Instead of `0xAbc...123`, you can send crypto to `alice.eth`.

These names:

- are yours forever (no annual renewal fees);
- can be sold as an asset;
- support nested records (addresses for different networks, email, etc.).

---

### 6. Real-World Assets (RWA)

Gold, real estate, art — all of this can be tokenized using NFTs.

A single NFT can represent:

- a share in an apartment;
- a gold bar in a vault;
- a right to part of a film's profits.

This makes expensive assets accessible: you can buy 0.1% of an apartment's value as a token rather than the whole thing.

---

## Risks and What to Keep in Mind

### The Bubble

In 2021–2022, the NFT market overheated. Pictures with no real value sold for hundreds of thousands of dollars — purely on hype.

👉 Many of them are worth zero today.

---

### Liquidity

Buying an NFT is easy. Selling one is often hard.

Unlike cryptocurrencies (which always have a market), NFTs are one-of-a-kind items. Your token could wait months for a buyer.

---

### Scams

- Fake collections (copying a popular project and disappearing).
- Phishing websites.
- Forged NFTs (someone "minting" another person's work without permission).

---

### Gas (Fees)

On Ethereum, creating, buying, and selling NFTs costs money (gas). During peak traffic, the fee can exceed the NFT's price.

👉 Solution: other blockchains — Polygon, Solana, Tezos — where fees are many times lower.

---

## Conclusion

NFTs aren't just "million-dollar pictures." They're a technological standard for **digital uniqueness**.

They're already being used for:

- tickets and passes;
- in-game items;
- certificates and documents;
- domains;
- tokenization of real-world assets.

Yes, the market went through a bubble, and many projects won't survive. But the concept of a **non-fungible token** itself isn't a passing fad. It's a way to record on the blockchain: "this digital object exists in a single copy."

And every year, there are more and more of these records.

## FAQ

### Does an NFT have to be a picture?
No. An NFT can be anything: music, text, an in-game item, a ticket, a certificate.

### Can I just copy the image from an NFT?
Yes, the file can be opened in a browser. But the owner is the person who holds the token in their wallet.

### Are NFTs bad for the environment?
On Ethereum, it used to be energy-intensive, but after the switch to Proof-of-Stake (September 2022), energy consumption dropped by ~99.9%.

### Should I buy an NFT right now?
Like any asset — only if you understand why you want it. For speculation, there are more liquid instruments.
