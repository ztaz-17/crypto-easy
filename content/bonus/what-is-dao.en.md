## What Is a DAO: A Company With No Director

---

Picture a company where:

- there's no CEO;
- there's no board of directors;
- there's no staff making all the calls.

And yet it runs, makes money, and grows.

Sounds like science fiction?

> **DAO (Decentralized Autonomous Organization)** — that's exactly what we're talking about.

A DAO is a decentralized autonomous organization run not by people, but by **code and member voting**.

In this article we'll cover:

> - what a DAO is in plain English;
> - how it actually works;
> - real-world examples: MakerDAO and Uniswap.

---

## What Is a DAO in Simple Terms

### Definition

A DAO is an organization that lives on a blockchain and is run by **smart contracts**.

Instead of managers — **rules written in code**.
Instead of orders — **token-weighted voting**.

Every DAO member can:

- propose changes;
- vote on decisions;
- influence the project's direction.

### How a DAO Is Different From a Regular Company

| Criterion               | Regular Company             | DAO                                    |
|-------------------------|-----------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Who's in charge?        | CEO / board                 | Community of token holders             |
| Rules                   | In documents                | In smart contracts (code)              |
| Transparency            | Closed reporting            | All decisions and transactions on-chain |
| Participation           | Employees only              | Any token holder                       |
| Changing the rules      | Management order            | Community vote                         |

---

## How a DAO Works

The governance process in a DAO has three steps:

### 1. Proposal

Any member creates a proposal to change protocol parameters.

For example:

- change protocol fees;
- allocate funds for development;
- add a new feature.

### 2. Voting

Token holders vote "for" or "against."

> **Your voting power = your token count.**

The more tokens you hold, the more weight your vote carries.

### 3. Execution

If a proposal gets enough votes — **the smart contract automatically executes the decision**.

Nobody can:

- override the vote result;
- ignore the community's decision;
- tamper with the outcome.

👉 **Code is law.**

---

### What Is a Governance Token

A governance token is the digital share of a DAO.

Holding one gives you the right to:

> - vote on changes;
> - submit proposals;
> - take part in the protocol's life.

Examples of governance tokens:

- **MKR** — MakerDAO's governance token;
- **UNI** — Uniswap's governance token;
- **AAVE** — Aave's governance token.

## DAO Examples

### MakerDAO

**MakerDAO** is one of the first and most famous DAOs.

What they did:

- created the **DAI** stablecoin, pegged to the US dollar;
- govern the protocol through votes by **MKR** token holders.

How it works:

> - MKR holders vote on protocol parameters (e.g., interest rates);
> - the vote results update smart contract settings;
> - the protocol runs autonomously, 24/7, without human intervention.

> MakerDAO manages billions of dollars in assets — without a single employee in the traditional sense.

### Uniswap

**Uniswap** is a decentralized exchange (DEX) that also operates as a DAO.

What gets decided through voting:

> - fee distribution;
> - liquidity pool parameters;
> - platform development.

The governance token is **UNI**.

Any UNI holder can:

> - propose changes;
> - vote;
> - shape the future of the world's largest DEX.

## Pros and Cons of DAO

### Pros

- **Transparency** — all decisions are visible on the blockchain;
- **Decentralization** — no single point of failure;
- **Global participation** — anyone can join;
- **Automation** — code executes decisions without middlemen.

### Cons

- **Risk of code bugs** — a smart contract flaw can cost millions;
- **Low turnout** — many token holders don't vote;
- **Concentration** — large holders can dominate;
- **Legal uncertainty** — DAO status isn't recognized everywhere.

## A Real-World Story

In 2022, the **MakerDAO** protocol faced a situation:

part of its collateral (Circle's USDC) was at risk of being frozen.

What did the DAO do?

> - MKR holders gathered for a vote;
> - proposed changing the collateral composition;
> - voted;
> - the decision was executed by smart contracts.

👉 The whole process — from idea to execution — took days, not months.
And there was no "management" deciding for everyone.

---

## Conclusion

A DAO is a new way of organizing:

- without bosses;
- with transparent rules;
- with governance through voting.

It's not a perfect system. But it:

> - solves the trust problem;
> - removes middlemen;
> - gives everyone a say.

> **In a DAO it doesn't matter who you are. What matters is what you propose and how you vote on it.**

## FAQ

### Do I have to buy tokens to join a DAO?

Yes, you need governance tokens to vote. But you can still take part in discussions without them.

### Are DAOs legal?

The legal status of DAOs varies by country. Some jurisdictions (e.g., Wyoming in the US) recognize DAOs as legal entities.

### Can a DAO make mistakes?

Yes. If the community makes a bad decision or there's a bug in the code, the DAO can suffer losses. Example: The DAO in 2016, when a code flaw led to a $60 million loss.

### How is a DAO different from a regular forum?

On a forum you can talk. In a DAO you can **vote and change protocol parameters**.
