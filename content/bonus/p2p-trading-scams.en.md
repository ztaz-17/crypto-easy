## P2P Trading: Scam Schemes and How to Avoid Them

---

You found a great deal on Binance [P2P](/en/glossary/#p2p). A seller is offering USDT at 5% below market price. They quickly message you in the chat: "Sending the transfer now, wait." A minute later they send a screenshot of a bank transfer and demand you release the crypto.

You check your balance in the banking app — no money. But the screenshot looks real. The seller pressures you: "The transfer has already been sent, the bank is processing it, release the USDT or I'll file a complaint!"

This is the classic **fake transfer** scheme. And if you release the crypto — you'll never see it again.

In this article, we'll cover:

>- exactly how scammers cheat on P2P platforms;
>- the two main schemes: fake transfer and chargeback;
>- how to trade safely and not lose your money.

---

## What Is P2P Trading

[P2P](/en/glossary/#p2p) (peer-to-peer) is direct trading between people. No middlemen:

- you agree with the seller;
- you transfer money to their card;
- they send you [cryptocurrency](/en/glossary/#cryptocurrency).

The platform (Binance [P2P](/en/glossary/#p2p), Bybit P2P, OKX P2P) acts only as an **arbitrator** — it holds the seller's crypto in escrow until you confirm payment.

👉 Sounds safe. But scammers find ways to bypass the protection.

---

## Scheme 1. Fake Transfer

### How It Works

A scammer creates a sell order for crypto (often at an attractive price). When you agree, they:

1. initiate a transfer through a bank branch but **don't complete it**;
2. or send money from a card with **insufficient funds** (the transaction hangs);
3. or use Photoshop — make a screenshot of a fake transfer;
4. and start pressuring you: "I've sent it, give me the crypto."

### Why It's Dangerous

- A screenshot proves nothing — any kid can fake one.
- Even a "green" status in the instant payment system on a screenshot doesn't guarantee the money was actually sent.
- The scammer uses psychological pressure: "the transfer can't be cancelled," "I'll lose my money," "I'll file a complaint."

### Real Story

User Ivan found a USDT order on Binance P2P at 92 RUB (the market was at 97 RUB). The seller was verified — 50+ trades, 4.9 rating. Ivan transferred 50,000 RUB to the seller's card. The seller sent a screenshot of a USDT transfer and asked Ivan to confirm receipt in the app.

Ivan confirmed — and ended up with neither his money nor the crypto. The screenshot was fake, the seller's account — hacked.

👉 Bottom line: neither the platform nor the bank returned the money. A voluntary transfer — you confirmed the deal yourself.

## Scheme 2. Chargeback

### How It Works

This is a more advanced scheme that works with bank cards.

1. A scammer buys crypto from you, paying with a **bank card**.
2. You see the money has arrived in your account — and release the USDT.
3. Within 1–14 days, the scammer calls their bank and claims the transfer was **fraudulent**.
4. The bank initiates a **chargeback** — reverses the payment. The money is debited from your account back to them.

You're left with neither crypto nor money.

### Why It Works

- In many banks, chargeback is a legitimate procedure to protect customers.
- The bank doesn't care that you were selling crypto — to them it's just a disputed transaction.
- The platform won't help either: you confirmed receipt of payment, the deal is closed.

### Real Story

Seller Alice was selling USDT through a P2P platform. A buyer paid 200,000 RUB with a Tinkoff card. Alice checked her balance — the money was there, she released the crypto. Three days later, 200,000 RUB vanished from her account. The bank processed a chargeback based on the buyer's claim.

Alice contacted the platform's support — useless. She contacted the bank — "the transfer was disputed by the sender." The money never came back.

👉 Chargeback is the most dangerous scheme because you see real money in your account. But it's not yours.

## How to Avoid Getting Scammed: Safety Rules

### 1. Don't Release Crypto Until the Money Is on Your Balance

This is the golden rule. Check not the screenshot, but your **actual balance** in the banking app.

⚠️ Even a "processing" status is no guarantee. Wait until the money hits your account and becomes available.

---

### 2. Only Use Trusted Platforms

Safe P2P platforms:

- **[Binance P2P](https://p2p.binance.com/en)** — the largest, escrow system, arbitration available.
- **[Bybit P2P](https://www.bybit.com/fiat/trade/spot)** — also reliable, good protection.
- **[OKX P2P](https://www.okx.com/p2p)** — escrow and support.

All these platforms **hold the seller's crypto** until payment is confirmed. That's your main protection.

---

### 3. Check the Seller

Before the deal, look at:

- number of completed trades (100+ is better);
- rating (4.9–5.0);
- registration date (scammer accounts are often fresh);
- reviews in the trade chat.

But remember: **even a verified account with history can be hacked**. Scammers often buy old accounts.

---

### 4. Don't Fall for Below-Market Prices

If someone offers USDT at 3–5% below the market rate — that's a red flag. Scammers lure victims in with attractive pricing.

Compare:

- rates on Binance P2P;
- rates on Bybit P2P;
- the spread between the best orders.

A reasonable difference is 0.2–0.5%.

---

### 5. Don't Leave the Platform

Scammers often suggest: "Let's deal directly, no fees." Or: "Message me on Telegram, it's faster."

**Never agree.** Once you step outside the P2P platform — you lose the protection of arbitration.

---

### 6. Use Banks Without Chargeback

Some banks and payment systems **don't support chargeback**:

- instant payment system transfers (SBP/FPS) — chargeback is practically impossible;
- transfers to Sberbank cards — harder to reverse than Tinkoff or Alfa-Bank cards.

👉 Whenever possible, sell crypto with payment via instant transfer systems, not bank cards.

---

### 7. Record Video and Save Evidence

If the deal amount is large:

- screen-record the payment moment;
- save the transfer confirmation from the bank;
- take screenshots of the trade chat.

This won't save you from chargeback, but it helps during platform arbitration.

## What to Do If You've Been Scammed

If you've fallen victim to a scammer:

1. **Contact the P2P platform's support immediately.** The faster you act, the higher the chance the scammer's account gets frozen.
2. **Contact your bank.** Report a fraudulent transfer. In some cases the bank may help — but don't count on it.
3. **File a police report.** It's theft, plain and simple. The chances of finding the scammer are low, but nothing will happen without a report.
4. **Check if your account was hacked** (if the scammer acted under your name). Change your password, revoke suspicious sessions.

But the best medicine is **prevention**. Follow the rules above and you won't lose your money.

## Conclusion

P2P trading is a convenient way to buy or sell [cryptocurrency](/en/glossary/#cryptocurrency) directly, without intermediary banks. But it's the "human factor" that makes it vulnerable.

Two main scam schemes:

- **fake transfer** — no money, but a screenshot "exists";
- **chargeback** — money arrives, then disappears.

The protection is simple:

> **don't release crypto until the money is on your balance**;
> **use trusted platforms** (Binance P2P, Bybit P2P, OKX P2P);
> **don't fall for below-market prices**;
> **don't leave the platform**.

[Cryptocurrency](/en/glossary/#cryptocurrency) gives you financial freedom. But the responsibility for security is yours.

## FAQ

### What is P2P trading in simple terms?

It's direct buying and selling of cryptocurrency between people. The platform (Binance P2P, Bybit P2P) acts as an arbitrator and holds the seller's crypto until payment is confirmed.

### Can money be returned after a P2P deal?

Yes, if payment was made with a bank card — the buyer can initiate a chargeback. The money will be debited from your account. Transfers via instant payment systems (SBP/FPS) are practically impossible to reverse.

### What's the main sign of a P2P scammer?

A price noticeably below the market rate and/or pressure tactics: "the transfer has already been sent, release it faster."

### Is it safe to trade on Binance P2P?

Binance P2P is one of the safest platforms if you follow the rules: don't confirm the deal until you receive the money, don't leave the platform, and check the seller.

### What is escrow in P2P trading?

It's when the platform temporarily holds the seller's cryptocurrency. The buyer transfers money to the seller and confirms payment through the platform. Only then is the crypto unlocked.

### Can a chargeback happen a month later?

Yes, in some banks the dispute window is up to 120 days. But most chargebacks happen within the first 1–14 days.
